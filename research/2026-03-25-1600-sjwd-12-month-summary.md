# San Juan Water District (SJWD) — 12-Month Summary

**Question:** Summarize what happened in the past 12 months for San Juan Water District. Include anything new and noteworthy, explain big budget items, and suggest interesting topics for further community exploration.
**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Organizations:** SJWD

---

## Leadership Transition

The most consequential event of the past year was a full leadership transition at the top of the District.

- **General Manager Paul Helliker departed in August 2025.** At the August 14, 2025 meeting, the Board voted 3-2 to terminate Helliker's contract rather than retain him in an advisory role. Multiple Board members and incoming GM Adam Larsen cited concerns that Helliker was undermining the transition -- reportedly telling people the Board had "hired the most unqualified candidate" and frustrating relationships with partner agencies. Directors McRae and Tobin, along with Zamorano, voted to terminate; Costa and Rich voted to keep him. The Board paid severance per the terms of the existing contract.

- **Adam Larsen was appointed General Manager** at the same August 14 meeting, approved unanimously. Larsen had previously served as the District's Field Services Manager. He expressed excitement about setting "the vision and direction for the District for the next decade." By September, Fair Oaks Water District GM Tom Gray publicly congratulated Larsen, and multiple agencies signaled eagerness to work with the new leadership.

- **Director Dan Rich resigned** from the Board sometime after August 14 (he was present at that meeting but gone by September). This created a Division 1 vacancy.

- **George Machado was appointed** to fill the Division 1 Board vacancy at the September 17, 2025 meeting. He was the sole applicant, but Board members noted his extensive background as a former District employee. He was sworn in at the dais.

- **Board officer elections in December 2025** produced some drama. Director Tobin argued she had been passed over for the presidency due to a prior censure and that there was an informal agreement she would rotate into the role. After a break to review agreement notes, the Board elected Ted Costa as President (4-1) and Pam Tobin as Vice President (unanimous). The censure against Tobin was formally rescinded in January 2026 (Resolution 26-01).

## Retail Water Rates -- The Dominant Issue

The retail rate structure has been the single most debated topic at SJWD over the past 12 months.

### Rate Increases Adopted, Then Partially Canceled

- In **July 2025**, after extensive public hearings and multiple special meetings (May 20, May 29, July 29), the Board adopted a four-year retail rate schedule: increases of **1%, 2%, 2%, and 2%** (Resolution 25-10), passing 3-2 with McRae and Tobin opposed.

- A major concession was made first: the **$5.2 million retail groundwater production facility and its associated debt issuance were removed** from the budget and rate proposal at Board direction, significantly lowering the needed increases.

- A Prop. 218 expert, Michael Colantuono, was retained and provided a legal opinion that both the current and proposed rates comply with Proposition 218.

- In **December 2025**, Finance Director Donna Silva reported that actual FY 2024-25 revenues exceeded budget by approximately **$2.1 million per year** while actual expenses were lower than budgeted. This allowed the Board to **eliminate the January 1, 2026 retail rate increase entirely** (Resolution 25-15, unanimous). The District effectively achieved **"pay-as-you-go" status** -- meaning it can fund the Kokila Reservoir, Cavitt Stallman Pipeline, and Administration Building improvements without issuing debt or raising rates.

### Rate Structure Controversy

Despite the rate increase being canceled, a significant controversy over the rate *structure* continued throughout the year:

- **Retail ratepayer Meera Deshmane** has been a persistent and vocal critic, arguing that customers with smaller meters (3/4") are paying disproportionately more relative to 1" meter customers, and that gravity-zone customers subsidize pumped-zone customers. She presented data at multiple meetings and Finance Committee sessions.

- **Vice President McRae** championed a deeper rate structure analysis, pushing for a "roadmap" to study whether costs should be allocated differently between gravity and pump zones and between meter sizes. He pointed to the Kokila Reservoir and Cavitt Stallman Pipeline as large capital projects that primarily benefit the pumped pressure zones but whose costs are shared across all retail customers.

- **Staff and consultants pushed back**, noting that: (a) the "postage stamp" rate approach (uniform rates for all) is legally defensible and industry standard; (b) all residential meters up to 1" serve similar demand regardless of meter size; (c) the District is replacing all meters with 1" meters over time; and (d) changing the structure would require a new Prop. 218 process.

- A **January 14, 2026 workshop** was planned but then **canceled** when the Board voted 4-1 in December 2025 to eliminate the rate structure analysis project, reasoning that the next full rate study (starting FY 2027/28) would be the appropriate time to revisit the structure, especially with new state conservation regulations expected in about 3 years. McRae cast the lone No vote.

- Conservation regulation implications loom large: the District may face a **23% reduction in water use** requirement if current litigation challenging those regulations is unsuccessful. This would likely force a rate restructuring toward tiered or conservation-oriented rates.

## Budget Overview (FY 2025-26)

The FY 2025-26 budget was workshopped in May 2025 and adopted in June:

- **Total budgeted revenue: $52.6 million**
- **Total budgeted expenses: $57.8 million**
- **Projected available reserves at year-end: $49.6 million**

The District operates four funds:

| Retail Capital | (various) | (various) |

### Key Budget Items

- **Capital spending is the largest expenditure** -- 21 wholesale projects and 30 retail projects planned for FY 2025-26.
- **Kokila Reservoir Replacement Project** -- the biggest single retail capital project. Construction contract awarded to Gateway Pacific Contractors in July 2025. The project will take approximately two years. It is financed partly through a **State Revolving Fund (SRF) loan**, with an interest rate set at 50% of the State's last debt issuance rate. During construction, the Bacon Pump Station will run at a set pressure, with no emergency storage available. Groundbreaking was anticipated in fall 2025.
- **Hinkle Reservoir cover/liner maintenance** -- The first annual cleaning of the Hinkle Reservoir cover was completed in 2025. The temporary tanks used during the Hinkle project are being removed (estimated cost $25-35K for demo, since they could not be resold).
- **Filter Backwash Hood Rehabilitation** -- South basin completed in 2025; north basin work began in December 2025, expected complete by May 2026.
- **Admin Building Deck Replacement** -- $288,750 contract with Kingsley Builders. Insurance covered $201,029; remainder split 50/50 between wholesale and retail.
- **Water Services Replacement Project** -- Service line replacements at Hidden Lakes Subdivisions 1 & 2 and the Granite Bay Shopping Center, awarded to Flowline Contractors.
- **CalPERS pension liability** -- The Board authorized a $200,000 voluntary payment in April 2025 to continue paying down the unfunded pension liability.
- **Salary and benefits** -- 11% increase in total personnel costs (approximately $800K salaries, $400K benefits), including costs for two temporary full-time employees for Lead and Copper Testing and Cross Connection programs.

### Mid-Year Review (April 2025, for FY 2024-25)

All four funds tracked favorably:
- Wholesale Operations: revenue above budget, expenses below -- unexpected $1M transfer to capital reserves.
- Retail Operations: revenue 2.4% above budget, expenses 3.9% below -- anticipated $3.7M transfer to capital reserves.
- Of 41 retail capital projects, 10 complete, 8 in progress, 4 expected to start, 14 expected to start and finish, 3 rolled over.

## Major Infrastructure and Water Supply

### Kokila Reservoir Replacement
The single largest infrastructure project underway. The existing Kokila Reservoir serves the upper pressure zones via the Bacon Pump Station. With the reservoir offline for two years during replacement, there is no emergency storage for those zones. The SRF loan agreement was finalized in September 2025. As of February 2026, significant grading was complete and no blasting was required.

### Hinkle Reservoir Temporary Tanks Removal
The temporary tanks installed during the Hinkle Reservoir rehabilitation are being removed. The Folsom pipeline underneath has been fully re-established. A construction contract for removal was approved in March 2026 with Resource Environmental, Inc.

### Hinkle 2 (Second Reservoir)
The District is investigating building a second reservoir on Bureau of Reclamation property south of Hinkle. This would provide emergency storage, allow continued operations when Hinkle is offline, and support future cover/liner replacements. Negotiations with the Bureau are on hold pending the Parcel C lease resolution.

### Long-Term Warren Act Contract (LTWAC)
The District's Warren Act Contract with the Bureau of Reclamation -- required to receive Placer County Water Agency (PCWA) water through Folsom Dam -- expired in February 2026. After years on temporary extensions, a new **25-year contract** was negotiated and approved by the Board in January 2026. The environmental assessment received no formal comments. The contract was expected to be signed in late February 2026.

### PCWA Raw Water Pipeline
PCWA completed a study on a raw water pipeline to deliver water to the City of Roseville (originally designed as 30-inch). SJWD is inquiring whether a **36 or 42-inch pipeline** could be installed to allow an additional 20 MGD delivery capacity to San Juan.

### Conjunctive Use and Groundwater Banking
This has been a major, complex, and sometimes contentious topic:
- The Sacramento Regional Water Bank Program Committee approved a starting balance for the groundwater bank that is **47% less** than what agencies had accumulated historically.
- The District has been advocating for **federal acknowledgment** (NEPA process) of the water bank, which would allow CVP water to be banked outside the service area.
- SJWD has been working with SSWD on a **backup supply agreement**.
- The District is exploring **percolation ponds** -- a consultant from GSI Water Solutions visited the District in early 2026 to evaluate feasibility.
- President Costa has pushed for a **standard water contract for banking** to streamline arrangements with any agency in the basin.
- The District is exploring whether it can receive credit for water released to **Baldwin Reservoir** and downstream via Cripple Creek.

### Water Forum Agreement 2050
The Board authorized the General Manager to sign the **Water Forum Agreement 2050** in December 2025 (unanimous). This is a voluntary agreement related to the regional Healthy Rivers and Landscapes Program. The District's participation includes paying a cost-share; the program has an eight-year enforceable term and provides funding mechanisms that reinvest fees back into local projects.

## Litigation

**Citrus Heights Water District et al. vs. San Juan Water District** (Case No. 23WM000064, Sacramento County Superior Court) has been an ongoing item at virtually every Board meeting, discussed in closed session. This litigation involves wholesale water rates charged to the District's wholesale customer agencies (Citrus Heights, Fair Oaks, Orange Vale). No public details on the status have been shared in open session.

The District is also a **lead plaintiff in litigation challenging state conservation regulations**, which if unsuccessful could require a 23% reduction in water use by the District.

An **anticipated litigation** item also appeared on several closed session agendas throughout the year, suggesting potential additional legal matters.

## Organizational Changes

- **Position reclassifications** (October 2025): Eliminated one Customer Service Technician and added a Human Resources Specialist; replaced the CMMS/GIS Coordinator with an Operational Technology Coordinator; eliminated one Water Efficiency Technician and added a Distribution Operator II; replaced Purchasing Agent with Procurement Specialist.
- **Title and salary adjustments**: Greg Zlotnick was promoted from Water Resources Manager to **Director of Water Resources & Strategic Affairs** with a salary range of $183,456-$220,147. Teri Grant's position was changed from Administrative Assistant/Board Secretary to **Clerk of the Board/Executive Assistant**. The meter positions were restructured into a three-tier classification (Meter Technician I, II, III).
- **Director of Finance salary**: Donna Silva's position was found to be 6% below market, but a full adjustment would have put her above the GM's salary. The Board approved a 3% increase now, with future adjustment tied to the next compensation study.
- **Legal Affairs Committee** was converted from a standing committee to an ad hoc committee in February 2026.
- **Board compensation**: An ordinance amendment (26-01) was introduced to increase the number of compensable days from 8 to 10 per month.

## Topics Worth Exploring Further

1. **The rate structure fairness debate**: Are small-meter and gravity-zone customers subsidizing pump-zone customers? The "postage stamp" vs. zone-based rate argument is unresolved and will return when the next rate study begins in FY 2027/28. This directly affects what ratepayers pay.

2. **Conservation regulations and their impact**: If the state's conservation requirements stand (23% water use reduction), it could force a fundamental restructuring of how the District charges for water. The District's litigation challenging these regulations is a high-stakes gamble.

3. **The Helliker departure**: The circumstances of the former GM's termination -- allegations of undermining the new GM, frustrating Board initiatives, and damaging inter-agency relationships -- are significant for understanding the District's internal dynamics and how they may have affected policy decisions over recent years.

4. **Wholesale rate litigation with Citrus Heights and Fair Oaks**: This long-running legal battle (since at least 2023) with the District's own wholesale customer agencies has significant financial implications. What are the wholesale agencies disputing, and what would a resolution look like?

5. **Groundwater banking and water market potential**: The District is positioning itself as a regional player in groundwater banking -- seeking federal acknowledgment, exploring percolation ponds, pursuing partnerships with SSWD, OVWC, and others. The revenue potential from water transfers and banking could be substantial.

6. **The Kokila Reservoir project and emergency water supply**: For two years during construction, upper-zone customers have no emergency storage. What is the District's contingency plan?

7. **The proposed Hinkle 2 (second reservoir)**: Building a second wholesale reservoir on Bureau of Reclamation land would be a major capital undertaking. How much would it cost, and how would it be funded?

8. **Healthy Rivers and Landscapes Program costs**: The multi-billion-dollar statewide program will impose fees on all water agencies. What will SJWD's share be, and how will it affect ratepayers?

9. **ACWA's captive insurance concept**: The Board received a presentation in February 2026 about creating a captive insurance company to invest reserve funds in equities rather than bonds. This is an unusual financial strategy for a public water district.

10. **Pay-as-you-go achievement**: The District reached a notable financial milestone -- the ability to fund major capital projects without issuing debt. Is this sustainable, and what does it mean for long-term rate stability?
