# Sacramento Metropolitan Fire District -- 12-Month Summary

**Question:** Summarize what happened at Metro Fire over the past 12 months, including anything new and noteworthy, big budget items, and ideas for community-interest topics to explore further.
**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Organizations:** SMFD

---

## Overview

The Sacramento Metropolitan Fire District (Metro Fire) had an eventful 12 months from roughly April 2025 through March 2026. The defining story is the launch of the Measure O general obligation bond program -- the largest capital investment in the district's history -- alongside a tightening fiscal outlook driven by slowing property tax growth and rising pension costs. The board also navigated leadership transitions, an emergency at its own headquarters, major vehicle fleet purchases, a new ambulance services contract, and continued work on behavioral health response.

Metro Fire is the seventh-largest local fire agency in California, serving roughly 720,000 people across 359 square miles of Sacramento and Placer Counties. Fire Chief Adam A. House leads the organization. The nine-member Board of Directors transitioned leadership at the end of 2025: John Costa (Division 9) became Board President, replacing Ted Wood (Division 4), with Robert Webber (Division 3) as Vice President and Brian Rice (Division 7) as Secretary.

## FY 2025/26 Budget: First to Exceed $400 Million

The FY 2025/26 Preliminary Budget was presented and adopted at the June 12, 2025 board meeting. Key figures:

- **Total budget across all funds: $459.1 million** -- the first time the district exceeded $400 million.
- **General Fund expenditures: $336.3 million**, up $8.2 million (2.5%) from the prior year.
- **General Fund revenues: $335.8 million**, up $10.4 million (3.1%).
- **General Fund deficit: $4.3 million**, to be covered by drawing down reserves.
- **General Fund reserve projected at $33.2 million (9.8%)**, well below the 15% target.
- **Labor costs: $271.2 million** (80.6% of General Fund revenues) -- the dominant expense category.

### Revenue Highlights

- **Property taxes: $222.5 million** (66.1% of General Fund revenue). Growth rate slowed to 4.2%, down from 5.0% the prior year, with the five-year forecast projecting growth at or below 4% going forward. High interest rates and suppressed home sales are the primary factors.
- **EMS/Medic transport revenue: $50.4 million**, up 4.9%.
- **PPGEMT (Medi-Cal transport reimbursement): $25.5 million gross, $19.2 million net** after state payments.
- **VRRP (Medi-Cal/uninsured transport): $15.3 million gross, $8.0 million net**, down 5.6% due to lower health plan participation.
- **Community Risk Reduction fees: $6.4 million**, up 7.7%.

### Expense Highlights

- **Wages grew $4.3 million (5.1%).**
- **Constant staffing overtime: $28.7 million**, down $2.6 million thanks to expansion of the firefighter reserve pool, but still elevated relative to pre-pandemic levels.
- **CalPERS Safety Plan contributions: $64.8 million**, up $6.3 million (10.9%).
- **Total retirement benefits: $73.9 million.**
- **Medical benefits (employee and retiree): $38.1 million**, up 6.0%.
- **Workers' compensation: approximately $12 million** (including all related costs).
- **Services and supplies: $60.1 million**, up $2.0 million, including increased dispatch services costs and computer services/licensing.

### Staffing

The budget added 10 new positions: 6 firefighters (starting May 2026), 1 shop assistant, 1 facilities tech, 1 helicopter mechanic, and 1 warehouse supervisor. Total budgeted full-time positions grew from 735 to 739. A reserve pool of up to 24 firefighters and 6 paramedics/EMTs is also being filled as part of the long-term service delivery plan. One firefighter academy and one metropolitan medic program academy were funded.

## Measure O: $415 Million Bond Program Launches

Measure O, a general obligation bond approved by voters on November 5, 2024, authorized up to $415 million for fire station construction, expansion, vehicle/apparatus acquisition, and a training facility. This is the biggest capital story for Metro Fire in years.

- **First bond issuance: up to $160 million in FY 2025/26.** The financing plan was adopted at the May 22, 2025 meeting. Resolutions were approved authorizing the execution of the Measure O Financing Plan, with KNN Public Finance as financial advisor and Stradling Yocca Carlson & Rauth as bond counsel.
- **An Ad Hoc Committee** for Measure O implementation and strategic planning was established at the June 12, 2025 meeting.
- **Multi-Site Fire Station Design Package 1** was awarded to COAR Design Group at the February 12, 2026 meeting, beginning the design process for multiple station projects.

## Capital Improvement Program: $150.4 Million Over Five Years

The five-year CIP (FY 2025/26 through FY 2029/30) was adopted June 12, 2025. It includes 46 projects totaling $150.4 million, with $109.0 million budgeted for the first year alone.

### Key Capital Projects

**New Fire Stations:**
- Station 112 Expansion: $22.0 million total ($1.15M in FY 25/26 for design)
- Station 23 Expansion: $22.0 million total ($1.15M in FY 25/26)
- Station 42 Relocation: $13.8 million total ($1.15M in FY 25/26)
- Station 61 Expansion: $16.4 million total ($1.15M in FY 25/26)
- Station 67 Buildout: $15.6 million (carryover, fully budgeted)

**Training Facility:**
- Zinfandel Training Facility Phase 3: $15.5 million (carryover, funded by a $13M state allocation)

**Major Vehicle/Apparatus Purchases:**
- Type I Engine Replacement: $19.7 million (FY 25/26)
- 25 North Star Ambulances: approved November 13, 2025
- Ladder Truck Replacement: $6.3 million (FY 25/26)
- Type III Engine Replacement: $5.6 million (FY 25/26)
- Type V Engine Replacement: $2.4 million (FY 25/26)
- 4 BME Type III Engines: approved November 13, 2025
- 7 BME Type V Engines: approved November 13, 2025
- Water Tenders (3000 gallon): approved November 13, 2025 and February 12, 2026

- Ambulance replacements, HazMat unit, rescue unit, dozer, decontamination unit, and more

**Funding mix for FY 2025/26 CIP:** 74.8% Measure O Building Fund, 11.7% Special Projects (Zinfandel), 11.5% Development Impact Fees, 1.8% General Fund, <1% Capital Facilities Fund.

## Headquarters Emergency: 10545 Armstrong Avenue

Starting in early 2026, the district declared an emergency at its headquarters at 10545 Armstrong Avenue in Mather. The exact nature of the emergency is not fully detailed in the agendas, but the board repeatedly authorized emergency contracting without competitive solicitation for "repair, demolition, and clean-up services required to restore 10545 Armstrong Avenue to a condition safe for occupancy." This appeared on the amended agenda for February 12, 2026, and was renewed at both the February 26, 2026 and March 26, 2026 meetings as a continuing emergency. This is an ongoing situation worth monitoring.

## Behavioral Health Response Program

The district continued expanding its behavioral health response capabilities. A fourth update (Behavioral Health Response Update #4) was presented at the October 23, 2025 meeting. Since February 2025, the district has been collecting detailed behavioral health data on calls. This program represents Metro Fire's growing role in mental health crisis response alongside traditional fire/EMS services.

## Citizen Responders Project

A new "Citizen Responders Project" was presented at the November 13, 2025 meeting by Captain James Ellis, Community Relations Specialist April West, and Director of Government Affairs Kyle Macdonald. This appears to be a community engagement initiative to train residents in emergency response skills.

## New Ambulance Services Contract with AMR

At the May 22, 2025 meeting, the board adopted a resolution awarding a contract to American Medical Response (AMR) for 9-1-1 Advanced Life Support ambulance services. AMR was the highest-scoring proposer. This contract governs the partnership between Metro Fire and AMR for ambulance surge capacity and mutual support.

## Fire Code and Safety Updates

- **2025 Fire Hazard Severity Zone Map:** Ordinance 2025-01 was introduced May 22, 2025, and adopted June 12, 2025.
- **2025 California Fire Code adoption:** Ordinance 2025-02 was introduced October 23, 2025, and adopted November 13, 2025.
- **PFAS-Free Firefighting Foam:** The district approved purchase of PFAS-free Class B foam (November 13, 2025), reflecting the nationwide transition away from PFAS-containing firefighting chemicals.
- **Hazardous Materials MOUs:** Agreements with both Amador County and Sacramento County for hazmat response team services were approved.

## Five-Year Financial Outlook: Key Risks

The Fire Chief's budget message identified several significant fiscal risks:

1. **Reserves below target:** The five-year forecast shows reserves averaging 10.3%, well below the 15% target. Building reserves will require expense reductions or new revenue sources.
2. **Rising pension costs:** CalPERS contributions increasing 10.9%, with unfunded pension liabilities a persistent challenge. The district also makes annual payments to Sacramento County Employees' Retirement System through ~FY 2036/37.
3. **OPEB (retiree medical) only 26% funded** after 11 years of prefunding.
4. **Federal EMS revenue at risk:** The $27.2 million net revenue from PPGEMT and VRRP programs could face federal budget cuts. If the federal government reduces funding and the state does not backfill, the district would need to adjust its budget significantly.
5. **Slowing property tax growth:** Projected to stay at or below 4% for the next four years.
6. **General Fund deficit:** The FY 2025/26 budget draws $4.3 million from reserves, and the forecast shows expenditures outpacing revenues in multiple years.

## Topics Worth Exploring Further

Here are several angles that would be of interest to the Fair Oaks community and broader Metro Fire service area:

1. **Measure O Implementation Progress:** How are the bond proceeds being spent? What is the timeline for the four new/expanded fire stations (112, 23, 42, 61) and when will they be operational? What impact will they have on response times in the community?

2. **The Armstrong Avenue Headquarters Emergency:** What happened at the district headquarters that required emergency repairs? How much will it cost? Is there temporary displacement of staff? This has been on the agenda for three consecutive meetings and could be a significant unplanned expense.

3. **Pension and Benefits Sustainability:** With CalPERS contributions at $64.8 million and rising, total retirement costs at $73.9 million, and OPEB only 26% funded, how sustainable is the district's long-term financial position? What are the implications for service levels?

4. **Federal EMS Revenue Risk:** What happens to Metro Fire's budget if the PPGEMT or VRRP federal programs are reduced or eliminated? The $27.2 million net revenue represents roughly 8% of General Fund revenue -- losing it would be significant.

5. **Behavioral Health Response Program:** How is Metro Fire's behavioral health response program evolving? What types of calls are being handled? How does this integrate with county mental health services and the Mobile Integrated Health agreement?

6. **Citizen Responders Project:** What does this new community program involve? How can residents participate? What kind of training is offered?

7. **Firefighter Workforce Health:** Multiple industrial disability retirements were processed in the past year. What do the trends look like for firefighter injury and disability? How effective are the wellness, EAP, and peer support programs?

8. **PFAS Transition:** The district is buying PFAS-free firefighting foam. What are the implications for environmental contamination at current and former fire stations? Is there remediation needed?

9. **Ambulance Fee Study and Ordinance Process:** The December 2025 presentation on ambulance and first responder fees could lead to fee changes. What are current fees and how do they compare to other jurisdictions?

10. **Labor Negotiations:** Six bargaining units are actively negotiating with the district. What are the key issues? How will outcomes affect the budget? The classification and compensation study with Gallagher may signal upcoming changes.

11. **Fire Station 67 Progress:** This is the largest single new construction project at $15.6 million. When will it be operational? What area does it serve?

12. **Zinfandel Training Facility:** What is the status of this $15.5 million training center funded by the state? When will it be completed and what capabilities will it offer?
