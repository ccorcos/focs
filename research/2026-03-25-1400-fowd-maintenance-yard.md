# FOWD Corporation Yard Redevelopment: Both Sides of the Debate

**Question:** Help me understand the water district maintenance yard issue — both sides of the argument along with facts and quotes.
**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Organizations:** FOWD

---

## Background

The Fair Oaks Water District (FOWD) has operated a maintenance/corporation yard at 10317 Fair Oaks Boulevard — in the heart of Fair Oaks Village — for decades. In 2006, when FOWD built its current administrative building, community leaders participated in planning a future redevelopment of the adjacent corporation yard. In 2018, the FOWD Board hired Gutierrez & Associates for $195,000 to design the project. The plan went through the County Planning Advisory Committee (CPAC) and Sacramento County Planning Commission, receiving development plan approval after roughly four years of entitlement work — much of which occurred during COVID.

By 2020, the estimated project cost was **$7 million** (August 2020 board meeting). By early 2023, public opponents were citing a **$10 million** figure, which General Manager Tom Gray disputed: *"I do not know where the $10 million came from"* (Feb 2023). Improvement plans were still being developed at that time, with no final engineer cost estimate or construction bids yet completed.

The issue erupted into a major public controversy starting in **December 2022**, when resident and Village business owner **Leon Corcos** challenged the project at a board meeting. Over the next three months (Jan–Mar 2023), public comment sessions drew large crowds — **92 people** attended the February 2023 meeting — overwhelmingly opposing the yard's redevelopment on that site. Despite this, the FOWD Board voted **unanimously (5-0) on March 20, 2023** to proceed with the project.

The controversy escalated into a **recall effort** against Board President Chris Petersen (Division 1) and Director Michael McRae (Division 2) in 2024. The recall **failed** in the November 2024 election. Following the election, Director Sarkovich stated he would like the Corporate Yard Project completed within two years (Nov 2024).

## Side 1: Against the Yard (Move It Out of the Village)

### Core Arguments

**1. Wrong use of prime real estate.** Opponents argue the Village's most visible corner should serve the community, not house an industrial maintenance yard.

> *"Why is a prime piece of real estate being used as a utility yard? ... It's a terrible use of prime real estate in the Fair Oaks Village."* — Leon Corcos quoting Sacramento County Supervisor Rich Desmond (Dec 2022)

> *"You are stealing the Village and giving me a gravel pit."* — Leon Corcos (Dec 2022)

**2. The Village has outgrown the yard.** Residents argue Fair Oaks Village is experiencing a renaissance and the yard doesn't fit the community's trajectory.

> *"The yard does not fit what they are striving for, it distracts from everything they have become and are becoming. The yard belongs somewhere but not here."* — Deborah Stark, representing younger families (Feb 2023)

> *"Towns and villages are either stagnating, thriving, or dying. To some extent, it is due to the opportunities and availabilities of amenities in the hub. Citrus Heights and Orangevale do not have a hub."* — Rick Tower (Feb 2023)

**3. Collaboration opportunities exist.** Opponents point to potential partnerships with the Park District and Sacramento County for shared yard space elsewhere.

> *"Mike Aho from the Park District also mentioned that he would be interested in finding some land outside of the core Village area to share with the Water District because they too need space for their maintenance and utility yard."* — Leon Corcos (Dec 2022)

**4. Cost concerns and rate hikes.** Critics tie the yard project to a 25% water rate increase over three years, questioning whether ratepayer money is being wisely spent.

> *"She is concerned that the estimated $10 million for the construction of the new yard will end up being more. She would like FOWD to justify their spending, raising the rates to potentially 25% within the next several years."* — Deborah Stark (Feb 2023)

> *"In 2006 FOWD had 40 employees and 31 in 2023, that's a 25% decrease in staff. And yet FOWD wants to make a gigantic building like their staff tripled in size."* — Visitor/Corcos (Jan 2023)

**5. Lack of transparency and public process.** Critics contend the CPAC process happened during COVID with minimal real public engagement.

> *"All that was completed during COVID, and it was challenging to go to CPAC meetings."* — Leon Corcos (Dec 2022)

> *"If this project had been brought out to the public, there would be a lot of people outside. The majority of people do not want the construction of the yard."* — Unknown visitor (Feb 2023)

> *"He feels there was not a significant amount of communication and transparency."* — Visitor Corcos (Jan 2023)

**6. Safety and quality of life.** Residents cite dump trucks, parking congestion, noise, and dust as problems the yard creates in a pedestrian-oriented village.

> *"There is nothing more terrifying than cycling to the Village and getting caught behind a dump truck or trying to dodge people ferociously looking for parking."* — Alison Kalsbeek (Feb 2023)

**7. FOVEC unanimous opposition.** The Fair Oaks Village Enhancement Committee formally opposed the yard's location.

> *"On behalf of FOVEC board, they unanimously asked for the maintenance yard to be moved out of the Village."* — Nan Danford, 2023 FOVEC President (Feb 2023)

**8. No independent analysis.** Opponents claim no independent needs analysis or rate increase study was conducted.

> *"He expressed his concerns about being unable to locate an independent needs analysis for the proposed Corporate Yard Project as well as an independent rate increase analysis regarding the validity of the 25% water rate increase over three years."* — Leon Corcos (Apr 2024)

## Side 2: In Favor (Redevelop on the Current Site)

### Core Arguments

**1. Long-standing plan with community input.** FOWD argues this vision was developed with community leaders starting in 2006, not imposed unilaterally.

> *"The Village leadership was primarily involved in the vision of building the FOWD Administrative Building and the future development vision of the FOWD Corporate Yard — what it would look like. The vision was established in 2006 and FOWD is implementing that vision now."* — General Manager Tom Gray (Dec 2022)

> *"Jim Racy [a long-term community pillar] saw that FOWD's vision for the development of the property was a great investment in the Village. ... The Browns, the McWilliams, a lot of people from the community, long time investors in the vision of the community, founding members of FOVEC were participants in the vision."* — Tom Gray (Dec 2022)

**2. Operational necessity — don't split staff.** FOWD leadership argues that separating operations and management staff across two sites would be inefficient and compromise emergency response.

> *"He doesn't want to split staff geographically."* — Director Sarkovich (Aug 2021)

> *"He emphasized the importance of the FOWD being in the central area to provide immediate services during emergencies."* — Director Marx (Mar 2023)

**3. The existing building is inadequate.** A 2004 study recommended demolishing the old yard building, which was originally a lumber yard — not designed for water district operations.

> *"The current building was designed as a lumber yard and not designed to run the water district."* — Director Sarkovich (Dec 2018)

**4. FOWD has the lowest rates in the region and zero debt.** The Board argues the District is financially responsible and the rate increase is for transmission main replacement, not the yard.

> *"FOWD has the lowest water rates in the entire Sacramento Region and over 100% less than the San Juan Water District, over 80% less than the Carmichael Water District and about 70% less than the Citrus Heights Water District."* — General Manager Gray (Feb 2023)

> *"FOWD has incurred no debt."* — General Manager Gray (Feb 2023)

> *"FOWD has reserved money for this project and did not raise rates for this project. FOWD raised rates primarily to rebuild the two transmission pipelines."* — Director Sarkovich (Dec 2022)

**5. Extensive entitlement process already completed.** Four years of planning, CPAC review, and County approval represent significant invested time and money.

> *"This ship has sailed a long time ago. When FOWD built the Administrative Building, it was decided that the Corp Yard would be refurbished in the future. FOWD hired an architecture firm and it took four years to go to CPAC and Sacramento Planning Commission; four years to get entitlement."* — Director Sarkovich (Dec 2022)

**6. FOWD is a good neighbor and community asset.** Supporters point to FOWD's track record of community investment: the administrative building, hosting the Historical Society, providing after-hours public parking, and maintaining their properties well.

> *"FOWD has one of the best producing wells north of the American River, right in the Village. FOWD reached out to the Fair Oaks Historic Society, which did not have a home, and offered them to home in a FOWD owned property to benefit the community."* — General Manager Gray (Feb 2023)

> *"FOWD is like an institution, and she appreciates it and is in favor of the Spanish Motif."* — Visitor Bramson (Aug 2024)

**7. Selling or vacating the land won't automatically benefit the Village.** At least one supporter warned about zoning and economic realities.

> *"If this project is not built and the FOWD is forced out, they will end up with an empty lot, it will not make economic sense."* — Visitor Wes Trunnell, citing the Fair Oaks NPA and SPA zoning (Feb 2023)

**8. The yard has always been part of the Village's character.**

> *"The yard has been part of the Fair Oaks Community that everyone fell in love with in the first place."* — Director Dolby (Mar 2023)

> *"He lives one-hundred yards across the street, has been here for thirty years."* — Director Marx (Feb 2023)

**9. Opposition is driven by a small group with self-interest.** Board members allege the campaign is primarily driven by one business owner/developer who wants FOWD's land.

> *"The Board decided to deny visitor Corcos' request to move the corporate yard and build a parking lot as his motivation was driven by self-interest. This is the only reason we are here."* — Director Sarkovich (Aug 2024)

> *"He is curious to know what percentage of FOWD's customers are asking for a Customer Advisory Committee. It seems to be the 1% that is badgering the District, those involved in the 'move the yard campaign.'"* — Unknown visitor (Aug 2024)

**10. Needs analysis was performed.** The Board asserts a needs analysis was completed by the project architects.

> *"FOWD is building a structure that meets its specific needs. A 'needs analysis' was done by Gutierrez & Associates (G&A) in 2018."* — Director Sarkovich (Mar 2023)

## Unresolved Questions

1. **What is the current cost estimate?** The $7M figure is from 2020; opponents cite $10M; final improvement plans and construction bids have not been publicly documented in the available minutes.
2. **Has FOWD explored relocation costs?** General Manager Gray asked opponents to provide evidence that relocation is cheaper, but no formal FOWD-commissioned relocation study appears in the record.
3. **What is the current project status?** As of November 2024, Director Sarkovich wanted it done within two years, but detailed construction timelines are not in the available minutes.
4. **What happened to the Park District collaboration idea?** Mike Aho's interest in shared yard space was mentioned in Dec 2022 but does not appear to have been formally pursued.
