Enrollment
CCCA’s financial condition depends on its ability to meet enrollment and attendance
projections. Enrollment and attendance are the driving factors underlying the Charter’s
largest single source of funding, the LCFF revenue. As was the case for 2023-2024,
CCCA’s enrollment for 2024-2025 is significantly lower than anticipated resulting in
$462,645 less in LCFF revenue.

For 2025-2026, CCCA projects enrollment to increase from 115 to 200 students based
on the addition of a grade level and additional recruiting efforts. However, if enrollment
only increases by 50 percent, to 175 students, LCFF revenue would be over $360,000
less than projected for 2025-2026, resulting in an ending fund balance of $330,950.

Fundraising
CCCA’s budget continues to be heavily reliant on fundraising. The budget assumes
$1,006,872 in donor contributions during 2024-2025. This is significantly more than the
$667,696 raised in 2023-2024. Through the end of January 2025, CCCA raised
$485,100 in donor contributions for the year and is expecting to receive an additional
$500,000 in grants and contributions in March for a total of $985,100. For 2025-2026,
CCCA reduces its projection of donor contributions to $435,000.

Borrowing
In 2023-2024, CCCA obtained a $250,000 operating loan from the California School
Finance Authority which will be repaid from 2024-2025 through 2027-2028. CCCA also
received short-term loans that have subsequently been paid off. CCCA has acquired a
$300,000 line of credit for 2024-2025 which it intends to pay back before the end of the
year. However, its cashflow projections anticipate that it will need to take out another

$300,000 loan in order to maintain positive cashflow through the last quarter of the year
and continue to borrow on a short-term basis to remain solvent in 2025-2026.

Notice of Concern: Year-End 2023-2024 and 2024-2025 First Interim
On February 7, 2025, SCOE sent a letter of concern regarding the CCCA’s low reserves
and continued heavy reliance on contributions from donors to remain solvent. The MOU
between the Sacramento County Board of Education (County Board) and CCCA
requires CCCA to maintain a Reserve for Economic Uncertainties equal to the greater
of five percent of General Fund Expenditure and other financing uses for the Charter
School. However, CCCA’s actual ending fund balance at the end of the 2023-2024
fiscal year was negative $215,983. We also noted that CCCA’s 2023-2024 audit report
indicates that, because CCCA held deficit cash and net asset balances as of June 30,
2024, a substantial doubt exists about the CCCA’s ability to continue as a going
concern.

Looking Ahead
As enrollment is lower than anticipated for 2024-2025, CCCA’s fiscal condition remains
precarious. If enrollment does not significantly increase next year, it will need to
significantly reduce staffing and expenditures and increase donor contributions in order
to pay its loans and remain solvent.

In CCCA’s 2023-2024 Draft Audit Report, the auditor raised concerns about CCCA’s
fiscal sustainability. The Audit Report includes the following language:

     The 2023-2024 negative fund balance/net assets was significantly impacted by
     the delay of anticipated grant funding until the following year (2024-2025). The
     subsequent year, the Charter has seen these grants actualized. The Charter has
     also been aided by significant philanthropic support by its industry partners and
     additional grants not previously budgeted. Currently, during the 2024-2025 fiscal
     year, the Charter is operating at a surplus stabilizing the cumulative fund
     balance/net assets. The Charter’s ability to restore its net asset balance to a
     surplus is mainly dependent on its ability to be successful in management’s
     plans; however, there have been no assurances that the Charter will be
     successful in its efforts.

Notice of Concern: Teacher Credentialing (2024-2025)

Annually, the Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CTC) notifies county offices of
education of teacher misassignments and vacancies from the previous school year in
districts and charter schools located within their jurisdiction. This information is reported
through the CalSAAS. Charter authorizers serve as the monitoring authority for their
charter schools, and as part of SCOE’s monitoring duties, it communicates with and
provides technical assistance to charter schools authorized by the County Board to
review and resolve teacher misassignments and vacancies.

In September 2024, SCOE conducted a review of 2023-2024 educator assignment data
in CalSAAS. SCOE initiated a proactive review of 2024-2025 teacher assignments to
assist its charter schools in addressing potential misassignments. The objectives of this
review were to:

   1. Reduce the number of misassignments reported in CalSAAS and on state
      dashboards,
   2. Ensure students in SCOE-authorized charter schools receive high-quality
      instruction from appropriately credentialed teachers, and
   3. Strengthen the capacity of charter schools to meet their charter obligations
      related to teacher staffing.

In October 2024, to support real-time resolution of teacher misassignments and
vacancies, SCOE requested CCCA’s current-year teacher roster, assignment records,
course descriptions, and credential documentation. Upon review of documents provided
by CCCA, CTC online resources, and communications with CCCA staff, SCOE
identified credential compliance issues for current CCCA educators.

Of CCCA’s eleven teachers, SCOE was concerned with misassignments and
credentialing issues for eight teachers. After meeting with CCCA’s Executive Director
Kevin Dobson and Director of Curriculum and Instruction John Pellman on October 23,
 Capital College and Career Academy Annual Report                                                    9
2024, SCOE assisted and verified the correction of two misassignments and one
correction in-process. On October 25, 2024, SCOE provided CCCA with a Notice of
Concern for the remaining two teacher misassignments and credentialing issues for
three educators. SCOE continued to work with CCCA to resolve noncompliant teaching
assignments, including providing guidance on immediate required actions, options for
temporary remedies, resources, and pathways to proper credentials and documents.
To date, based on the information received from CCCA, SCOE has verified that six of
the teacher assignment issues have been addressed by obtaining appropriate CTC
documents, reassigning teachers, obtaining a Temporary County Certificate, and
removal of misassigned teachers. SCOE continues to seek clarification and information
from CCCA to resolve the two remaining issues.

While SCOE has offered technical assistance and support on several occasions,
CCCA’s responses and continued actions have demonstrated a need for better
understanding regarding some credential requirements, including the need for a teacher
to possess an appropriate authorization prior to assignment, the use of interns, and
special education requirements.

4.       FACILITIES UPDATE

CCCA’s school site utilizes traditional classrooms and includes features such as a
shop/maker space where students create CTE projects, science labs, technology
spaces, student-designed gardens, and collaborative learning spaces with flexible
furnishings. HVAC work has been completed and new portable classrooms have been
added. Additionally, spaces have been identified for a street soccer court and weight
training facility for students. The site has been developed in order to accommodate
future growth as remaining grade levels are added.

The CCCA team decided not to move forward with the Boys and Girls Club satellite site
due to transportation issues. CCCA is working with American River College (ARC) to
identify a satellite space on the ARC campus for students attending dual enrollment
courses at ARC.

Professional Development and Team Approach to Student Learning

CCCA has developed and implemented a professional inquiry process highlighted by
their Professional Learning Communities (PLC) focus on the school’s Pillars:
Collaboration, Responsibility, Grit, Critical Thinking, and Leadership. Additionally, the
staff engages in annual goal setting as a team and addresses student achievement
through an interdisciplinary, team approach. This model allocates time for whole group
staff meetings, PLCs, and individual professional inquiry projects. In this first operational
year, with all new staff members, PLCs were focused on foundational teaching and
 Capital College and Career Academy Annual Report                                          13
classroom management support. Teachers were encouraged to engage in self-
reflection, setting professional goals and collecting evidence of their growth, supported
by a structured staff development model.

Additionally, CCCA has a variety of ways to meet the social-emotional needs of its
students. For example, CCCA contracts with Neighborhood Wellness, which provides
adult mentors at no cost to support students, including during lunch and passing
periods. CCCA also works with STRIVE Community Health for counseling services
through their interns. STRIVE interns support students in academic and social-
emotional matters, including Restorative Practices. As of October 2024, STRIVE
provided four counselors for student support.
