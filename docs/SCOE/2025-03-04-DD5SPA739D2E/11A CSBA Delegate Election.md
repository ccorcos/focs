         13. What do you see as the biggest challenge facing governing boards and how
             can CSBA help address it? *

                  I see declining enrollment and academic/fiscal accountability as the biggest challenges facing
                  governing boards and public education. One source of this epidemic is the degree of openness to
                  varying perspectives and different ideas, as well as the asking of vital questions. The absence of
                  critical dialogue puts the education system and local education agencies at risk. If individuals do
                  not exist in an environment that welcomes different ideas and spurs debate, the LEA's risk
                  becoming ineffective or constrained in their purpose; and ultimately the public-school model
                  suffers.

                  Other highly impactful challenges are rapidly changing technology, an uncertain economy,
                  changing state demographics, and increased focus on non-academic topics. We now see many of
                  these factors manifesting in California test scores. I also believe certain policies that do not
                  consider rights of parents, students, and faculty together are leading many to abandon the public
                  school experience for other options such as homeschool, private school, or are just leaving the
                  state altogether. This seems to be occurring in a way not existing in the past as many are left
                  feeling unheard. I believe the culture of the CSBA can help drive these crucial, honest -- and
                  sometimes spirited -- debates that are needed in today's educational arena.

                  As one example, my husband and I were ardent voices regarding certain COVID-related policies
                  that are now openly acknowledged and observed at a national level as damaging to students'
                  learning with minimal true risk to them. My husband and I instinctively recognized the potential
                  risk to public education and learning. We spoke out passionately to bring light to these potential
                  impacts; not only in education, but in the community at large, even though these were highly
                  unpopular views at the time. In a similar vein, we also instinctively recognize critical patterns as
                  they emerge. We willing to ask key questions and offer our support to decision-makers.
