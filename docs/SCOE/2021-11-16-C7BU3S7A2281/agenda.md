Public Comments\
Public comments may be made via the Zoom Q&A Tool. Once the meeting has
started, click the Q&A button at the bottom of the Zoom window. When the
Q&A window appears, enter your name and the agenda item you wish to
address in the input box, then choose \"Send.\" Be prepared to speak
when prompted by name during the public comments portion of the meeting.
Public Comments will be received until the public comment period has
elapsed. Comments are limited to a maximum of two (2) minutes per
speaker per agenda item, and individuals are limited to a single comment
per agenda item. You may contact boardcomments@scoe.net prior to the
meeting if you have questions about this process.\
\
Anyone may submit public comments to the Board on any item that is
within its subject matter jurisdiction. However, the Board may not take
action on any item not on the agenda except as authorized by Government
Code section 54954.2. Anyone may provide public comments in support of
or in opposition to any item being presented for consideration.\

Accommodating Individuals with Disabilities\
The Sacramento County Office of Education encourages those with
disabilities to participate fully in the public meeting process. If you
need a disability-related modification or accommodation, including
auxiliary aids or services, to participate in the public meeting,
contact the Superintendent's Office at (916) 228-2410 at least 48 hours
before the scheduled Board meeting so that we may make every reasonable
effort to accommodate you. \[Government Code § 54953.2; Americans with
Disabilities Act of 1990, § 202 (42 U.S.C. § 12132).\]\

Mission Statement\
The mission of the Sacramento County Office of Education (SCOE) is to:\
• ensure that our students are prepared for success in college, career,
and community;\
• provide educational leadership to the diverse groups we serve;\
• work creatively and collaboratively with partners; and\
• give educators and support staff the training and tools they need for
success.

[[The California Department of Education (CDE) has developed a
comprehensive plan to provide technical assistance for continuous
improvement to Local Education Agencies (LEAs). The Title III County
Office of Education Regional English Learner Specialists Agreement Grant
is funded through the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA). As
part of the California Statewide System of Support, the CDE awarded
grants to one county within each of the state's eleven California County
Superintendents Educational Services Association (CCSESA) regions. The
Sacramento County Office of Education (SCOE) was selected in Region 3 as
the Regional English Learner (EL)/Title III
Specialist.]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

 

[[As the Region 3 EL Specialist, SCOE will provide technical assistance
for continuous improvement to equip LEAs and COEs with resources and
tools to support the development, implementation, and evaluation of
strategies to ensure that English Learners have the resources they need
to succeed. The scope of work includes guidance and support to LEAs and
COEs in the implementation and improvement of programs as well as the
development of Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) Addenda. In
addition, SCOE's Curriculum Specialist for English Learner Programs and
Accountability will organize, provide coaching for systemic improvement
in data analysis, and data-based decision-making, high-quality
instruction, connect resources for specialized programs, and facilitate
quarterly Multilingual Education Leadership Network meetings to support
LEAs.
]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

[[The Sacramento County Board of Education (Board), on its own and in
its capacity as County Board and Sacramento County Committee on School
District Organization, has held meetings via teleconference from April
2020 through September 2021 in compliance with Governors\' Executive
Orders, which relaxed certain legal requirements for Board meetings
during the COVID-19 pandemic. The legal requirements for public meetings
are set forth in Government Code section 54950, et. seq., which is known
as the "Brown Act." The Executive Orders expired on September 30,
2021. However, recently enacted Assembly Bill 361 amended elements of
the Brown Act to permit local agencies to continue holding
teleconferenced meetings using simplified procedures during a proclaimed
state of
emergency.  ]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

 

[[Under Government Code section 54953(e), in order to continue holding
teleconferenced Board meetings using simplified Brown Act procedures,
local agencies, including school districts and county boards of
education, must make the following findings by majority vote and
readopt such findings every 30 days
thereafter:]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

1.  [[The Board has reconsidered the circumstances of the state of
    emergency,
    AND]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}
2.  [[The state of emergency continues to directly impact the ability of
    members to meet safely in person, OR state or local officials
    continue to impose or recommend measures to promote social
    distancing.]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

[[In the event the Board decides to continue to hold teleconferenced
Board meetings using abbreviated Brown Act procedures, Resolution No.
21-15 is attached for the Board\'s
consideration. ]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

[[**BACKGROUND:**]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

 

[[The California Department of Education estimates that approximately
30% of the state\'s K-12 classrooms are at least 50 years old and 10%
are 70 years old. In addition to health, safety, and normal wear and
tear, schools need to be updated to meet 21st century educational needs
and environmental efficiencies. Studies have found a positive
relationship between the condition of school facilities and student
achievement. There are significant student improvements in standardized
test scores, attendance rates, and student effort following attendance
at a new or modernized school
facility.]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

 

[[California voters passed the last statewide School Bond (Proposition
51) in 2016, however, funds from this Bond are already fully obligated.
As a result, the state Office of Public School Construction has created
a 'wait list' which has over \$3 billion worth of unfunded school
facilities grants on it. Assembly Bill 75, scheduled for the ballot in
2022, another statewide school facility bond, worth \$12 billion will
fund projects on the 'wait list' and future needs. Furthermore, AB 75
will not raise individual local property taxes, but will reduce the need
for additional local property taxes for school
facilities.]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}

 

[[SCOE is eligible to receive and has applied for \$8.8 million in
reimbursements from the state for the construction of Nathaniel S.
Colley, Sr. High School. However, as indicated above, there currently is
no state bond funding available to reimburse SCOE. The school facility
bond to be placed on the ballot by AB 75 will fully fund the \$8.8
million in reimbursements for
SCOE.]{style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"}]{style="font-size:14px;"}
