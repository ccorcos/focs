BACKGROUND
On March 18, 2025, the Miracle University Charter High School (MUCHS) submitted a
petition to establish a countywide charter school. The initial petition did not have
sufficient parent or teacher signatures. On April 28, 2025, MUCHS supplemented and
completed its petition by submitting the required number of teacher signatures.

MUCHS proposes a countywide charter high school, proposing that “authorizing
MUCHS’ petition will result in the opening of multiple school sites across Sacramento
County by a proven team of school leaders.” The petition states that, as a countywide
charter, MUCHS can scale up quickly, open more sites, and serve more students.
(Petition, p. 23.)

MUCHS’s mission “is to ensure all public high school students in Sacramento County
graduate with a high school diploma, character of service, purpose, and critical thinking
skills, as well as gain admission to either a college or a career technical school.
(Petition, pp. 9, 29.) Through the MUCHS program, petitioner will strive to end the
“dropout crisis…starting in Sacramento County.” (Petition, p. 15.)

MUCHS intends to address the dropout crisis through the “Miracle Formula.” This
formula has two foundational pillars - Handz on Mentors and the CREATE Teaching
Model. Under the mentorship component, each student is promised a culturally
responsive mentor, with each mentor serving 40-50 students, while checking in with
each student on a daily basis to monitor their attendance, behavior, classwork, and
wellness, making frequent home visits, speaking students’ home languages,
establishing impactful connections, and building students’ confidence. The CREATE
Teaching Model proposes to equip teachers with the determined mindset and practical
teaching strategies that unlock the potential from every child. (Petition, p. 12.)

Petitioner’s Past History

Dr. Kadhir Rajagopal, the Lead Petitioner (Petitioner) and Founding Director of the

STANDARD OF REVIEW FOR COUNTYWIDE CHARTER PETITION
The law favors charter authorization by local school districts. For a charter petition
submitted to a local school district, the presumption is in favor of granting the charter.
The school district shall not deny a charter petition unless it makes written factual
findings with specific facts that show the petition does not meet one or more legal
requirements. (Ed. Code, § 47605(c).)

The standard for approving a countywide charter school is higher than for a district
authorized charter. The County Board may approve a countywide charter petition only if
it is satisfied that:

    •    approving the charter is consistent with sound educational practice;
    •    the charter school has reasonable justification for why it could not be established
         by a petition to a school district; and
    •    the educational services to be provided by the charter school will offer services to
         a pupil population that will benefit from those services and that cannot be served
         as well by a charter school that operates in only one school district in the county.
         (Ed. Code, § 47605.6(a)(1),(b).)

In addition, the County Board shall deny a countywide charter petition if it finds one or
more of the following:

    1. The petition does not contain the required number of parent or teacher
       signatures.

    2. The petition does not contain an affirmation of each of the conditions
       described in Education Code section 47605.6(e) [e.g., nonsectarian, not charge
       tuition, not discriminate, admit all students who wish to attend, satisfy public
       random drawing requirements, etc.].

    3. The petition does not include a declaration of whether or not the charter school
       shall be deemed the exclusive public school employer of the employees of the
       charter school for purposes of the Educational Employment Relations Act
       (EERA). (Ed. Code, § 47605.6(b)(6).)

    4. The charter school presents an unsound educational program for the pupils to
       be enrolled in the charter school.

    5. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the
       program set forth in the petition.

    6. The petition does not contain reasonably comprehensive descriptions of all
       the elements listed in Education Code section 47605.6(b)(5)(A)-(P).

In addition to the higher standard for approval, the County Board has more discretion to
deny a countywide charter than a district charter. The County Board may deny the
petition for a countywide charter on any other basis that it finds justifies denial.
(Ed. Code, § 47605.6(b)(7).) It may also impose any additional requirements that it
considers necessary for the sound operation of a countywide charter school. (Ed. Code,
§ 47605.6(b).)

        C. FINANCIAL ANALYSIS

One of the most crucial elements in assuring that the charter school program can be
successfully implemented, along with competent staff, is a realistic financial and
operational plan. (ARR 2400, Appendix A, § C.3.)

   1.    Summary of Financial Plan and Budget

MUCHS’ financial plan and budget are set forth in Appendix 6 of its Petition.

MUCHS proposes the operation of a charter high school focusing on dropouts and
students at risk of dropping out of school beginning in the 2025-2026 school year. It
plans to begin with grades 9-12 and 250 students enrolled during the first year, growing
to 600 students by its fifth year. MUCHS projects budget surpluses and positive fund
balances throughout the five-year term of the petition. However, these balances are not
sufficient to maintain positive cashflow throughout this period. As a result, MUCHS has
obtained a $1.5 million line of credit which it will need in each of its first five years of
operation to maintain positive cashflow.

   2.    Enrollment Growth Projections

MUCHS anticipates 250 students enrolled in grades 9-12 at the beginning of its first
year of operation. By year five, MUCHS projects that 600 students will be enrolled,
implying an approximate 25% compound annual enrollment growth rate over the term of
the proposed charter.

The enrollment projections appear to be based on a claimed waiting list of over 1,000
students for Miracle University’s dropout prevention program and private school, as well
as MUCHS’ ability to build capacity over the term of the charter.

Using the waiting lists for its current private school programs to project enrollment for
the new charter suggests that Miracle University is seeking to convert its private school
into a public charter, which is in violation of Education Code section 47602(b). In
addition, SCOE’s experience is that only a fraction of families who express interest in a
charter school actually enroll their children in the school. Enrollment has been declining
in many school districts in Sacramento County, while districts have been expanding
their programs to attract and retain students. Given this trend, MUCHS will face
heightened enrollment pressures and, if enrollment does not materialize as projected,
revenues would be significantly reduced, putting MUCHS at risk of insolvency. MUCHS’
projected ending fund balance for its first year of operation is not sufficient to absorb
more than eight less students enrolled than projected.

   3.    Local Control Formula Revenue

Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) revenue makes up the largest source of revenue
for the proposed charter, comprising approximately 87% of total revenue sources in the
first five years. This revenue source is dependent upon a variety of factors, but mainly
Recommendations and Findings – Miracle University Charter High School Countywide Charter Petition   14
enrollment and attendance, which makes these targets essential to meet or exceed.
An overestimation of these factors would significantly affect revenue and lead to
insolvency.

High-risk populations may attend school at a lower rate (65-75%) than an average high
school population (90-95%). (SCOE ARR 2400, Appendix B.) Therefore, the 95%
attendance rate stated in the petition is likely too high depending upon the actual
composition of the enrolled student body and the challenges of drawing students from
across Sacramento County to the main school site when no transportation will be
provided. We note that the attendance rate for the Natomas and Sacramento City
unified school districts where the charter proposes to operate and for other countywide
benefit charters authorized by SCOE ranges from 90-93%.

In addition, MUCHS assumes that over 86% of its students will be high needs (low-
income, English learners and/or foster youth), generating large LCFF Supplemental and
Concentration grants. This is based on SCOE’s enrollment of high needs students;
however, it is not clear if MUCHS’ enrollment will match SCOE’s. Note that the
enrollment of high needs students in the school districts within which the charter
proposes to operate and for other countywide charters authorized by SCOE ranges
from 68%-77%.

Based on the attendance rate and enrollment of high needs students in school districts
where the charter proposes to operate and for other countywide benefit charters
authorized by SCOE, MUCHS’ LCFF projections are overestimated by $300,000 to
$500,000 in its first year, with growing funding shortfalls each year thereafter. MUCHS’
projected ending fund balance for its first year of operations is not sufficient to absorb
this revenue shortfall.

   4.    Special Education Program Costs

The MUCHS budget includes one certificated support position and 3.5 instructional
aides in the first year growing to four support positions and 19 aides in the fifth year of
operation. The budget also includes $200,000 for special education services in the first
year of operation, but only approximately $66,000 in the third year of operation. Given
that the proportion of students with disabilities in its comparison school, Visions for
Education, is over 15%, MUCHS should expect approximately 37 students with
disabilities in year one and 60 students in year three. MUCHS does not increase
certificated staffing for students with disabilities during that time while simultaneously
reducing their contract costs for special education. The one certificated support position
and $66,000 included in the budget for the third year will not be sufficient to serve 60
students with disabilities.

Based on SCOE’s experience, the staffing and budget proposed for special education is
not realistic in any of the first three fiscal years. We estimate the cost to provide special
education services to MUCHS students in the first year is likely to be at least $400,000
growing to at least $640,000 by year three, depending on the number and needs of
students identified for special education services. MUCHS has not fully accounted for
these costs in its budget and, as a result, it has not provided a feasible financial plan to
provide an adequate program for students with disabilities.

IV. PROGRAM ANALYSIS

A charter petition must include a reasonably comprehensive description of all of the
required statutory elements. (Ed. Code, § 47605.6(b).) Each of these elements is
discussed below.

Review of the required elements is important because the County Board’s future oversight
of the charter school will be based on the charter petition itself, and the petition’s
description of the services promised to prospective students.

To be “reasonably comprehensive,” staff considered whether each statutory element
contained a description that includes information that is substantive; includes
elaboration; addresses all aspects of the required charter petition elements; is specific
to the charter petition being proposed; describes the program to a sufficient degree;
improves pupil learning; that increases learning opportunities; and for which
accountability and legal compliance can be reasonably foreseen and assured.

Mere quoting of the law or general statements of educational practices without detailed
information of how it will be accomplished by the petitioner do not constitute a
reasonably comprehensive description of the required element.

       A. ELEMENT A – DESCRIPTION OF A SOUND EDUCATIONAL PROGRAM

The County Board may approve a countywide charter petition only if it is satisfied that
granting the charter is consistent with sound educational practice. (Ed. Code, §
47605.6(b).)


Recommendations and Findings – Miracle University Charter High School Countywide Charter Petition                      21
The MUCHS petition has a worthy goal to end the dropout crisis. However, the petition
does not describe a sound educational program, as required by California standards, for
the pupils proposed to be served in the charter school. The CREATE Teaching Model,
which is the foundational academic pillar of the petition, lacks detail in several significant
areas that are critical to delivering a high-quality, research-based education, particularly
for the high needs student population the charter school intends to serve.

While the petition references curriculum, it does not describe how that curriculum will be
implemented to ensure all students meet or exceed academic standards. There is an
overall lack of depth in outlining instructional strategies aligned with current educational
research. Specifically, the petition does not demonstrate an understanding of how to
deliver instruction in ways that support diverse learners through multidimensional,
engaging approaches, as expected by the California Content Standards and Curriculum
Frameworks.

Notably, the petition fails to include essential systems and structures that are widely
recognized as research-based best practices in supporting student learning and
behavior. There is no mention of:

    •    Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): A comprehensive framework that
         integrates academic, behavioral, and social-emotional supports through multiple
         tiers of intervention. MTSS uses data-driven decision-making to provide
         increasingly intensive levels of support to meet the needs of all students.
    •    Positive Behavior Interventions and Supports (PBIS): A proactive approach
         to improving school climate and student behavior by teaching and reinforcing
         positive behaviors. PBIS promotes consistency in behavior expectations across
         all school settings and uses data to guide decision-making.
    •    Response to Intervention (RTI): An academic support model focused on early
         identification and intervention for students struggling with learning. RTI provides
         targeted instruction at increasing levels of intensity based on student need and
         ongoing progress monitoring.
    •    Whole Child Approaches to Learning: An educational philosophy that supports
         the development of every aspect of a child—including academic, social,
         emotional, and physical well-being—recognizing that learning is most effective
         when students feel safe, supported, and engaged.
    •    Professional Learning Communities (PLC): A collaborative model in which
         educators work in teams to analyze student data, share effective teaching
         practices, and engage in continuous improvement to enhance student learning
         and professional growth.
    •    Intervention Courses or Strategic Support Classes: Structured classes
         designed to provide targeted instruction to students who need additional help in
         specific subject areas, such as reading or math. These courses often take place
         during the school day and are aligned with students’ learning needs based on
         assessment data.

The charter application demonstrates a minimal understanding of how assessment of
and for learning should inform instructional practices. An effective educational program,
particularly for a high needs population, requires the strategic use of assessment data

Recommendations and Findings – Miracle University Charter High School Countywide Charter Petition   22
not only to evaluate student progress (assessment of learning) but also to actively guide
and adjust instruction (assessment for learning). Without a clear plan to modify and
differentiate instruction based on ongoing assessment, the program fails to meet the
anticipated significant academic needs of its students, many of whom will require
targeted interventions and personalized supports to succeed. (See sections IV.B and IV.
C for more detail).

The only described method of academic support beyond core instruction is mandatory
after-school tutoring and re-teaching, which is insufficient to meet the needs of all
learners, particularly those requiring targeted interventions during the school day. There
is no evidence of a comprehensive or proactive support system that ensures all
students are given equitable opportunities to access and engage with the curriculum.

Handz on Mentoring (HOM), which is a key feature of the Miracle approach, also raises
questions about the validity and feasibility of implementation. The petition promises that
each mentor will serve 40-50 students, check in with each of their students every day,
and monitor the attendance, behavior, classwork or grades, and wellness of every
student on a daily basis. Additionally, the petition promises that the mentors will make
regular home visits and speak the language of their mentees. (Petition, pp. 11, 35.)
The ability for each mentor to carry a caseload of 40-50:1, check in with and monitor
attendance, behavior, classwork, and wellness of each of their 40-50 students every
day, while speaking the home language and making regular home visits does not
appear realistic. While the description of these services is impressive, the petition does
not outline or provide sufficient detail to demonstrate the capacity to implement the
promised plan.

The charter petition also contains numerous impractical and inconsistent elements that
collectively illustrate a lack of a coherent and operational plan. Some discrepancies are
relatively minor, such as conflicting bell schedules—page 45 states the school day runs
from “8:30 am to 4:00 pm,” while Appendix 8 lists the regular day as “8:00 am–2:40 pm,”
which also violates SB 328 which requires the school day for high schools to begin no
earlier than 8:30 a.m. Examples of more significant issues include claims about a
“Miracle formula” that has transformed “thousands of potential dropouts into graduates”
(Petition, p. 10.) and “thousands of failing high school students into scholars” (Petition,
p. 14), yet no details are provided about what this “proven model” (Petition, p. 18)
entails, nor is any validated evidence included to support the results.

Additionally, the “One School, Multiple Campus” model (Petition, p. 26) raises logistical
concerns, including videoconferencing instruction across campuses with team-teaching,
which would require multiple staff at two locations. Transporting students between
campuses for extracurriculars would be costly and reduce instructional minutes. The
petition states that this model would “facilitate deep collaboration” and “increase access
to professional development programming” (Petition, p. 26), but it is not clear how this
would be achieved because the petition does not describe when or how such
collaboration or development would occur, particularly when “every teacher” is required
to provide after school tutoring.

In summary, the petition does not articulate a thoughtful, research-informed educational
program. The absence of key instructional strategies, support systems, and inclusive
practices raises significant concerns about the school’s ability to deliver on its mission
and meet the diverse needs of its anticipated student population. Also, as noted in the

The CREATE Teaching Model is not aligned with the 2023 California Mathematics
Framework (CMF), particularly in its approaches to assessment and student support.
Under the “Assess and Master in Class” pillar (Petition, p. 39), the model emphasizes
nearly constant checks for understanding, with teachers calling upon nearly every

topic. Students who do not pass the exit ticket must attend after-school tutoring the
same day. The “Test Models” component (Petition, p. 39) focuses on alignment
between teaching and assessments, ensuring students are only assessed on skills that
have been taught, excluding diagnostic exams used to establish baseline performance

identities and cultural backgrounds.

gaps remain. The description does not clarify how Reveal Math’s assessments and data

progress monitoring and mastery lacks connection to building a culture of belonging or
empowering students as active learners. Additionally, the CMF highlights the
importance of involving students in self-assessment and reflection to promote
intellectual risk-taking and reduce anxiety, especially for marginalized learners—
elements absent from MUCHS’ current approach. Without explicit strategies linking
assessment data to culturally responsive teaching and student engagement, the
assessment system risks failing to fully support equitable and inclusive mathematics
learning as envisioned in the CMF.

VI. COUNTYWIDE CHARTER SCHOOL ANALYSIS

        A. FINDINGS REQUIRED TO AUTHORIZE COUNTYWIDE CHARTER SCHOOL

A county board of education may approve a countywide charter only if it is satisfied
that:

    •    approving the charter is consistent with sound educational practice;
    •    the charter school has a reasonable justification for why it could not be
         established by petition to a school district;
    •    the charter school will offer educational services to a pupil population that will
         benefit from those services, and that cannot be served as well by a charter
         school that operates in only one school district in the county.

(Ed. Code, § 47605.6(a)(1),(b).)

        B. MUCHS’ PETITION DOES NOT JUSTIFY COUNTYWIDE CHARTER
           STATUS

The petition provides the following rationales for submission of a countywide petition:
1) the high school dropout rate is a countywide issue; 2) a countywide charter school
can be more rapidly scaled to serve more students; 3) a countywide charter is needed
to grant enrollment preference to residents of Sacramento County; 4) a countywide
charter will result in “operational efficiencies and economies of scale;” 5) a “One School
Multiple Campus” model maximizes academic benefits; and 6) a “One School Multiple
Campus” model supports the upward mobility of families in the County. (Petition,
pp.16-27.) None of these rationales demonstrate that students cannot be served as well
by a charter school that operates in only one school district or that the proposed charter
school could not be established by a petition to a school district.


Recommendations and Findings – Miracle University Charter High School Countywide Charter Petition   39
The statutory scheme of the Charter School Act reflects an intent to promote district-
chartered schools and local oversight while allowing for limited exceptions for
countywide charters upon required findings. (Ed. Code, § 47605, et. seq.; CSBA v.
State Board of Education (2010) 166 Cal.App.4th 1298, 1320.) Many charter schools
seek to address educational issues that apply throughout the county, but this does not
prevent their programs from being established as district-authorized charters. MUCHS’
petition does not show why and how its proposed educational program cannot be
provided by a petition to a school district and all its rationales are also available to
district-authorized charters.

While district authorized charters must give preference to students residing in the
district, both district and countywide charters may recruit and enroll students from
throughout the county. (Ed. Code, §§ 47605(e); 47605.6(e).) Therefore, there is no legal
or other barrier that prevents MUCHS from serving students from outside a school
district or from offering its proposed program as a district authorized charter school.

Most students attend school near their place of residence. MUCHS is not required to
provide transportation to its students and has no stated plans to do so. As the proposed
long-term school facility is in a business park at 2800 Gateway Oaks within the
Natomas Unified School District (NUSD), it is unlikely that many of the students MUCHS
is proposing to serve would be willing or able to travel from all parts of the county to this
school site. Instead, students would primarily come from within NUSD, which according
to DataQuest, has the highest cohort graduation rate and the lowest dropout rate in the
county. 7

MUCHS claims that only a countywide charter can rapidly scale to serve a greater
number of students and that “one school multiple campuses” maximizes academic
benefits, but the charter petition has no plans or budget for rapid scaling to multiple
campuses throughout the county. It plans to open one facility, neither of which is likely
to draw students from across the large geographic area of the county. In any event,
multiple campuses may be operated through district-authorized charter schools.

Finally, “operational efficiencies and economies of scale” are insufficient justification for
why a charter school could not be established by petition to a local school district. The
many district-authorized charters operated by Charter Management Organizations with
a network of charter schools across different school districts (e.g., Aspire, California
Montessori Project) is evidence that a countywide charter is not justified under this
rationale.

MUCHS’ petition does not demonstrate that students cannot be served as well by a
charter that operates in only one school district or provide a reasonable justification for
why it could not be established by petition to a school district. (Ed. Code, § 47605.6(b).)
Furthermore, as set forth in the analysis of the educational program above, the petition
does not support a finding that approving the charter is consistent with sound
educational practice. (Ed. Code, § 47605.6(a)(1),(b).) Therefore, it does not meet the
requirements to be approved as a countywide charter.

Reports of Misconduct

An employee who observes or has evidence of another employee's inappropriate conduct shall
immediately report such conduct to the principal or Superintendent or designee. An employee
who has knowledge of or suspects child abuse or neglect shall file a report pursuant to the

Reporting.

(cf. 5141.4 - Child Abuse Prevention and Reporting)

disciplinary action and, in the case of a certificated employee, may be subject to a report to the
Commission on Teacher Credentialing. The Superintendent or designee shall notify local law
enforcement as appropriate.

The Board of Trustees desires to enhance student learning by providing an orderly, caring, and nurturing educational
and social environment in which all students can feel safe and take pride in their school and their achievements. The
school environment should be characterized by positive interpersonal relationships among students and between
students and staff.
(cf. 0410 - Nondiscrimination in District Programs and Activities)
(cf. 3515 - Campus Security)
(cf. 3515.2 - Disruptions)
(cf. 5030 - Student Wellness)
(cf. 5131.4 - Student Disturbances)
(cf. 5142 - Safety)
(cf. 5145.3 - Nondiscrimination/Harassment)
All staff are expected to serve as role models for students by demonstrating positive, professional attitudes and
respect toward each student and other staff members. Teachers shall use effective classroom management
techniques based on clear expectations for student behavior.
(cf. 4119.21/4219.21/4319.21 - Professional Standards)
Staff shall consistently enforce Board policies and regulations which establish rules for appropriate student conduct,
including prohibitions against bullying, cyberbullying, harassment of students, hazing, other violence or threats of
violence against students and staff, and drug, alcohol, and tobacco use.
(cf. 0450 - Comprehensive Safety Plan)
(cf. 3513.3 - Tobacco-Free Schools)
(cf. 4020 - Drug and Alcohol-Free Workplace)
(cf. 5131 - Conduct)
(cf. 5131.1 - Bus Conduct)
(cf. 5131.6 - Alcohol and Drugs)
(cf. 5131.7 - Weapons and Dangerous Instruments)
(cf. 5136 - Gangs)
(cf. 5144 - Discipline)
(cf. 5144.1 - Suspension and Expulsion/Due Process)
(cf. 5144.2 - Suspension and Expulsion/Due Process (Students with Disabilities))
(cf. 5145.2 - Freedom of Speech/Expression)
(cf. 5145.7 - Sexual Harassment)
(cf. 5145.9 - Hate-Motivated Behavior)

         I. Scope and Purpose

              A. This policy recognizes legislative intent, as to the purposes of charter schools, to
                 provide a method to:

                      1. Improve pupil learning.
                      2. Increase learning opportunities for all pupils, with special emphasis on
                         expanded learning experiences for pupils who are identified as academically low
                         achieving.
                      3. Encourage the use of different and innovative teaching methods.
                      4. Create new professional opportunities for teachers, including the opportunity to
                         be responsible for the learning program at the schoolsite.
                      5. Provide parents and pupils with expanded choices in the types of educational
                         opportunities that are available within the public school system.
                      6. Hold the schools established under this part accountable for meeting
                         measurable pupil outcomes, and provide the schools with a method to change
                         from rule-based to performance-based accountability systems.
                      7. Provide vigorous competition within the public school system to stimulate
                         continual improvements in all public schools. (Education Code (Ed. Code),
                         section 47601)

              B. This policy governs the submission of petitions to the Sacramento County Board of
                 Education (Board) for operation of charter schools under the oversight of the
                 Sacramento County Office of Education (SCOE). This policy also governs:

                      1. Consideration and granting or denial of charter petitions by the Board.
                      2. Opening and operation of schools under approved charters.

         I. General Provisions

          These Administrative Rules and Regulations (ARR) pertain to the following types of charter
          petitions submitted to the Sacramento County Board of Education (Board):

              A. Appeal. Petitions that have been denied by the local governing board of a school
                 district in Sacramento County, and subsequently submitted to the Board for review,
                 per Education Code (Ed. Code, § 47605(k).);
              B. Sacramento County Office of Education (SCOE) Pupil Population. Petitions that
                 propose to serve pupils for whom the SCOE would otherwise be responsible for
                 providing direct education and related services, per Education Code section 47605.5;
                 or
              C. Countywide Charter. Petitions to establish a countywide charter school that will
                 operate at one or more sites within Sacramento County, provide instructional services
                 not generally provided by SCOE, and benefit pupils who cannot be served as well by
                 charter schools operating in only one school district, per Education Code section
                 47605.6.

          These ARR have been developed to carry out Board Policy (BP) 2400. These ARR provide
          guidance of the factors SCOE staff will consider in determining whether charter school
          petitions meet applicable standards; the process SCOE staff will use to provide
          recommendations to the Board regarding the granting or denial of charter petitions; the
          monitoring and oversight of charter schools under approved charters; and the procedures
          for renewal, non-renewal, or revocation of approved charters.

       Criteria for the Review of Charter School Petitions and Charter School Renewal Petitions
       by the Sacramento County Board of Education (Board).

            A. For purposes of Education Code section 47605(c), a charter petition shall be
               “consistent with sound educational practice” if, in the BOARD's judgment, it is likely
               to be of educational benefit to pupils who attend. A charter school need not be
               designed or intended to meet the educational needs of every pupil who might
               possibly seek to enroll in order for the charter to be granted by the BOARD.

            B. For purposes of Education Code section 47605(c)(1), a charter petition shall be
               “an unsound educational program” if it is any of the following:

                      1. A program that involves activities that the BOARD determines would
                         present the likelihood of physical, educational, or psychological harm to the
                         affected pupils.

                      2. A program that the BOARD determines not to be likely to be of educational
                         benefit to the pupils who attend.

                      3. If the petition is for renewal of a charter school, and either the charter school
                         has not met the standards for renewal pursuant to Education Code section
                         47607, et seq., as applicable, or the charter school has not met the
                         measurable pupil outcomes as described in its charter.

            C. For purposes of Education Code section 47605(c)(2), the BOARD shall take the
               following factors into consideration in determining whether charter petitioners are
               “demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program.”

                                           Charter School Petition Review Tips

       This Tip Sheet is designed to offer guidance to charter school petitioners filing an
       appeal or original petition with the Sacramento County Board of Education. Over the
       course of preparing recommended findings of fact for the County Board, Sacramento
       County Office of Education (SCOE) staff has found that many petitions lack some
       critical elements. The County Superintendent and designees suggest that petitioners
       consider the following tips and ensure that the petition addresses these common
       oversights before submitting a petition:
       Tip No. 1: Realistic Budget Figures.

                       The budget must align with the programs offered in the petition.
                        Frequently petitions promise programs and classes which lack a
                        corresponding expenditure. For example, if the petition states that the
                        charter school will offer a computer lab, the budget should reflect the costs
                        of the furniture and computer equipment and peripherals along with
                        ongoing costs for staff, utilities, licensing, etc. Additionally, the facility
                        should have space allocated for the lab.
       Tip No. 2: Consistency throughout the Petition.

                       The petition’s narrative concerning the programs offered and the timelines
                        in the petition should match with the budget and the program parameters.
                        For example, the number of days referenced for the school year should be
                        consistent with the number of days used to calculate revenues.

                       As another example, if the instructional plan lists textbooks to be used in
                        the classroom, the charter budget should include a plan to purchase those
                        textbooks.
       Tip No. 3: Use of Realistic Enrollment Patterns.

                       Frequently, petitions will utilize enrollment figures which assume maximum
                        enrollment on the first day of school with 100% attendance without data
                        support. When estimating the percent of enrolled pupils that attend daily
                        to earn ADA (average daily attendance), the petitioner needs to consider
                        what is reasonable for the expected pupil population. For example, in a
                        high risk population, pupils may attend 65% - 75% of the time, while an
                        average high school population would be 90% - 95% of the time.
                        Additionally, a more realistic estimate of enrollment for purposes of
                        calculating average daily attendance would start with fewer pupils and
                        then grow as the school becomes established. One way to reflect a
                        realistic enrollment pattern would be to include gradual growth that
                        eventually meets the goal enrollment. Petitioners may also demonstrate
                        realistic enrollment figures by documenting recent contact with pupils

                           (i.e. phone calls to parents to re-confirm meaningful interest in attending),
                           or detailed recruitment plans demonstrating the likelihood of achieving
                           enrollment targets.

       (Note: Expenditure patterns (especially in hiring teachers) would follow the same pattern)

       Tip No. 4: Awareness of Special Education Funding.

                       The petition should reflect an understanding of special education funding
                        which impacts the petition’s use of realistic budget figures and the
                        petition’s consistency (see Tips 1 and 2 above).

                       In preparing Recommended Findings of Fact, SCOE staff will consider
                        whether the petitioners are members of a Special Education Local Plan
                        Area (SELPA), or have provided evidence that the charter school has
                        applied for SELPA membership. Because each SELPA has its own
                        unique funding model, the SELPA should be identified at the time of the
                        petition to properly budget and allow staff sufficient information to evaluate
                        special education funding and expenditures.

                       Charter petition budgets should assume that special education
                        expenditures are the same for a charter school as for other public schools.
       Tip No. 5: Awareness of SELPA procedures, timelines, and responsibility for providing
       the full continuum of special education services.

                       Frequently petitioners have indicated a proposed charter school’s intent to
                        provide services through the Sacramento County SELPA, but the petition
                        reflects a lack of clarity related to the SELPA process.

                       Before opening and operating a charter school approved by the Board,
                        petitioners must demonstrate that the charter school has been accepted
                        as a member of a SELPA for purposes of providing special education
                        services, and that it is capable of providing the full continuum of special
                        education services.

                       Petitioners should apply for membership in a SELPA before or at the
                        same time they submit their charter petition to the Board (e.g., the
                        Sacramento or El Dorado County Charter SELPA). Petitioners should
                        consult with the SELPA that petitioners will apply to concerning its
                        timelines and acceptance processes.

                        state academic content standards or without specific, concrete plans on
