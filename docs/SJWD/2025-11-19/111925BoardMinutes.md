VIII.   DISCUSSION AND ACTION ITEMS

        1. Retail Rate Structure Analysis (R)
           Vice President McRae reported that the Finance Committee received a presentation
           from Ms. Meera Deshmane which was included in the Board packet. In addition, he
           informed the Board that she will provide a presentation at the January 14th workshop
           and he will also provide a presentation, which he plans to bring to the next Finance
           Committee meeting.

           Vice President McRae suggested that he reach out to some consultants who have
           already been through the process of setting tiered rates and have them join the
           workshop to provide a quick overview of their projects along with answering
           questions from the Board. Director Tobin agreed with the suggestion and would like
           to see how consultants would approach this and provide insight for the best business
           practice for this situation. The Board discussed having a consultant and agreed that
           if one can be available to provide more information to the Board that Vice President
           McRae should schedule them to attend.

           GM Larsen voiced concern that should the Board decide to adjust rates then a
           second Prop. 218 process will have to be instigated which might be confusing to
           customers. He pointed out that the Prop. 218 experts have confirmed that the
           District’s retail rates are justifiable and reasonable. In addition, he reminded the
           Board that the new conservation regulations will be coming down, in about 3 years,
           and, more than likely, the District will need to change the rate structure at that point;
           therefore, he recommends that the Board wait until then to change the rate structure.

           Ms. Silva pointed out that the District’s current “postage stamp” rate structure and
           your fixed vs. variable rate adjustments have been very effective at providing the
           District financial stability; however, it does not incentivize conservation and there are
           significant conservation requirements that the District may see in 3 years. She stated
           that the District will need to find a way to achieve compliance with the water reduction
           that will be required by the new laws. She commented that there are about 7 different
           rate structures that are being utilized and the Board should be educated on the pros
           and cons of each of those rate methodologies. Mr. Zlotnick pointed out that there is
           another issue which is the litigation that the District is a lead plaintiff on and if the
           litigation is successful then it is anticipated that the impact will be much lower with
           respect to required reductions in water use. Vice President McRae expressed his
           desire not to wait since there are customers who have presented the Board with their
           arguments about unjust water bills.

           Ms. Deshmane addressed the Board and believes there is a common theme of fear
           of change and she encouraged the Board to have a consultant provide their opinion
           of the rates. In addition, she disagreed with a statement previously made regarding
           similarly situated customers and their rates.

    Big Impact on a Small Budget:
    Grant Strategies for Resource-
          Strapped Agencies
Subject: Summary of ACWA Region 3 Webinar on Strategic Funding &
Grant Readiness
Prepared by: Adam Larsen
Date: November 19, 2025
                 Purpose of Report
• This report provides the San Juan Water District Board of Directors
  with a comprehensive summary of the recent ACWA Region 3
  virtual program, which focused on strategic approaches for water
  agencies, particularly those with limited staffing or financial
  capacity, to effectively identify, apply for, and manage grant
  funding.
• The session included practical guidance on capital planning, grant
  strategy, partnership development, and long-term funding
  alignment. The information is relevant to the District as we
  continue to pursue outside funding that supports infrastructure
  resilience, water supply reliability, and long-term financial stability.
                Program Overview
The webinar was moderated by ACWA Region 3 leadership and
included panelists from utility districts, engineering firms, and ACWA’s
federal relations team. The overarching goal was to help agencies
build internal capacity to successfully compete for federal and state
funding.
Program Focus:
• Strategies for resource-strapped agencies to maximize outcomes.
• Practical methods for finding grants, building partnerships, and
   strengthening organizational readiness.
• Long-term integration of grants into capital and financial planning.
  Key Strategic Recommendations
Develop a Multi-Year Capital Plan
• Prioritize the agency’s top projects based on need, readiness, and
   funding potential.
• Align capital improvements with known grant cycles and eligibility
   criteria.
• Ensure shovel-ready design and permitting where possible to
   improve competitiveness.
Craft an Organizational Story
• Develop a clear narrative that defines the District’s challenges,
   community benefits, regulatory drivers, and long-term goals.
• Use this narrative consistently across applications and legislative
   engagement.
  Key Strategic Recommendations
Establish a Grant Tracking System
• Monitor federal, state, and local funding programs (e.g., SRF,
   Bureau of Reclamation, wildfire defense grants).
Grant Writing Best Practices
• Use previous successful applications, internally or borrowed from
   peers, as templates.
• Tailor each project description to match the specific grant’s goals
   and language.
• Use strategic project titles incorporating terms such as resilience,
   infrastructure, and fire protection.
Grant Administration Capacity
• Post-award reporting and compliance are often more burdensome
   than the initial application.
• The program emphasized that grants are not “free money.”
   Administration costs time and staff capacity.
  Key Strategic Recommendations
Financial Planning Considerations
• Grants should supplement, not dictate, project selection or
   prioritization.
• Integrate grant strategy into long-term financial and capital plans.
• Plan proactively for local match requirements and communicate
   the need for flexible reserves.
Strengthen Relationships & Advocacy
• Engage early with legislators, appropriations staff, and agency
   grant managers.
• Pursue bipartisan relationships to maximize support for federal
   earmarks.
• Host facility tours, participate in regional water coalitions, and
   partner with other districts to amplify the District’s visibility.
  Federal & State Funding Insights
Federal Earmarks (Congressionally Directed Spending)
• Available to water districts and nonprofits.
• Typically, one-year funding with significant transparency, audit,
   and compliance obligations.
• May trigger Buy America requirements and potential NEPA review.
• Internal deadlines for congressional offices are often in March,
   making early preparation essential.
State Revolving Fund (SRF)
• California’s largest source of water infrastructure funding.
• Prioritizes disadvantaged communities, but other agencies remain
   competitive with well-justified projects.
• Offers both loans and grants depending on program guidelines.
Panel Takeaways Relevant to SJWD
• Be strategic, not opportunistic: Chasing every grant can dilute staff
  resources and reduce competitiveness.
• Readiness matters: Agencies with completed designs, permits, and
  well-developed metrics score higher.
• Partnerships multiply effectiveness: Regional collaboration
  improves funding success and reduces administrative burden.
• Communication counts: Thank-you notes, follow-ups, and
  consistent communication build credibility.
• Persistence is essential: Funding cycles are lengthy; preparation
  must begin well before application windows open.

• It is recommended that the Board receive and file this report.
• District staff is already implementing most of the recommended

  enhance these efforts wherever beneficial to support future
