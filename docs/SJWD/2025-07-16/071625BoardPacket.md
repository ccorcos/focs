   II.   PROPOSITION 218 HEARING
         1. Proposed Retail Water Rate Increase

             President Costa opened the Proposition 218 Hearing at 6:31 p.m.
                                                            June 11, 2025, Board Minutes
                                                                                 Page 2



Ms. Grant, SJWD Board Secretary, confirmed that the Proposition 218 Hearing was
properly noticed to all retail property owners as required by law. GM Helliker reported
that seven (7) written letters were received protesting the proposed retail water rate
increase. Copies of the letters and any received until the close of the hearing will be
attached to the meeting minutes.

President Costa directed Ms. Donna Silva, Director of Finance and Human
Resources, to proceed. Ms. Silva reviewed the staff report which was included in the
Board packet. She reviewed the background and timeline of the Retail Financial Plan
and Rate Study noting that the plan was reviewed at 10 Board meetings and
workshops before staff was directed to prepare the Prop. 218 notice indicating a
proposed 2% retail rate increase for each of the next 4 years. She informed the Board
that no action is being requested by the Board tonight and this item will be on the
agenda at the June 12th Board meeting.

Mr. Mark Hildebrand conducted the presentation, and a copy of the presentation will
be attached to the meeting minutes. He reviewed the information, providing details
as needed. In response to Director Zamorano’s comments on the Groundwater
Production Facilities debt financing, GM Helliker informed the Board that about three
years ago the Board directed staff to put this project in the budget for the upfront
payment to secure groundwater production capacity. President Costa clarified that
the funds would need to be borrowed. Ms. Silva explained that this is a placeholder
and the project is showing as an anticipated expense which would need to be debt
financed. GM Helliker explained that the Board directed staff to pursue partnerships
and if those attempts did not pan out then the Board would consider pursuing its own
well. In response to Vice President McRae’s question, Ms. Silva informed the Board
that the retail debt was $14.2 million at the end of December 2024 and is less than
that now as there have been two debt service payments since then.

Mr. Hildebrand informed the Board that they will be considering the rate increases at
tomorrow’s Board meeting and if approved then the first rate increase would be
effective July 1, 2025.

Ms. Meera Deshmane addressed the Board and voiced concern regarding the graph
in the Prop. 218 notice being incorrect and provided an explanation of how to
compute weighted average. She provided information regarding her analysis of the
retail water rates and the weighted average that the District used, which she feels is
incorrect.

In response to President Costa’s request, Mr. Hildebrand informed the Board that he
researched the background for the .75 weighted average back to 2006 and found a
footnote in a report that explained that the .73 weighted average (at that time) was
based on the weighted average of the meters within a group of customers. Mr.
Hildebrand stated that, in 2017, the weighted average was updated to .75 but with
no footnote explaining the change; therefore, he has been relying on that analysis
when performing subsequent studies, as there was no footnote to draw his attention

          good point which will be reviewed during the upcoming rate structure analysis.

BACKGROUND

Provisions of the California Health and Safety Code (Attachment 1) specify that larger (>10,000 service
connections) water utilities prepare a special report by July 1, 2025 if their water quality measurements
have exceeded any Public Health Goals (PHGs). PHGs are non-enforceable goals established by the
Cal-EPA’s Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA). The law also requires that
where OEHHA has not adopted a PHG for a constituent, the water suppliers are to use the Maximum
Contaminant Level Goals (MCLGs) adopted by the United States Environmental Protection Agency
(USEPA). Only constituents that have a California primary drinking water standard, such as a maximum
contaminant level (MCL), and for which either a PHG or MCLG has been set are to be addressed.
(Attachment 2 includes a list of all regulated constituents with the MCLs and PHGs or MCLGs.)

The law specifies what information is to be provided in the report. (See Attachment 1) If a constituent
with an MCL was detected in San Juan Water District’s water supply from 2022 through 2024 at a level
exceeding an applicable PHG or MCLG, this report provides the information required by the law. During
the 2022 – 2024 period, there were two constituents detected at levels above the PHG, or if no PHG,
above the MCLG. Included for each constituent is the numerical public health risk associated with the
MCL and the PHG or MCLG, the category or type of risk to health that could be associated with each
constituent, the best treatment technology available that could be used to reduce the constituent level,
and an estimate of the cost to install that treatment if it is appropriate and feasible.

WHAT ARE PHGS?

PHGs are drinking water constituent levels set by the California OEHHA, which is part of Cal-EPA, and
are based solely on public health risk considerations. None of the practical risk-management factors that
are considered by the USEPA or the California State Water Resources Control Board Division of Drinking
Water (DDW) in setting drinking water standards (such as MCLs) are considered in setting the PHGs.
These factors include analytical detection capability, treatment technology available, and benefits and
costs. The PHGs are not enforceable and are not required to be met by any public water system.
MCLGs are the federal equivalent to PHGs.

GUIDELINES FOLLOWED

The Association of California Water Agencies (ACWA) formed a workgroup that prepared guidelines for
water utilities to use in preparing this required report, updated in April 2025. The ACWA Guidelines were
used in the preparation of our report. The OEHHA Health Risk Information for Public Health Goal
Exceedence Reports, dated February 2025, was also consulted (See Attachment 2).

WATER QUALITY DATA CONSIDERED

All of the water quality data collected by our water system from 2022 through 2024 for purposes of
determining compliance with drinking water standards was considered. This data was all summarized in
our 2022, 2023, and 2024 Consumer Confidence Reports that were noticed to all of our customers by
June 2023, 2024 and 2025, respectively. (Attachment 3)

BEST AVAILABLE TREATMENT TECHNOLOGY AND COST ESTIMATES

Both the USEPA and DDW adopt what are known as Best Available Technologies (BATs), which are the
best known methods of reducing contaminant levels to the MCL. Costs can be estimated for such
technologies. However, since many PHGs and all MCLGs are set much lower than the MCL, it is not
always possible or feasible to determine what treatment is needed to further reduce a constituent
downward to or near the PHG or MCLG, many of which are set at zero. Estimating the costs to reduce a
constituent to zero is difficult, if not impossible, because it is not possible to verify by analytical means
that the level has been lowered to zero. In some cases, installing treatment to try and further reduce very
low levels of one constituent may have adverse effects on other aspects of water quality.

The following is a discussion of the constituents with California MCLs that were detected in our drinking
water at levels above the PHG, or if no PHG, above the MCLG:

Total Coliform Bacteria:

From 2022 through 2024 there were four months that had detectable total coliform in the distribution
system that was below the MCL, but exceeded the MCLG of zero. In May 2022, 53 samples were
collected and one sample had a total coliform positive detect, resulting in 1.88 percent of samples
positive. In July 2022, 46 samples were collected and two samples had a total coliform positive detect,
resulting in 4.35 percent of samples positive. In September 2022, 43 samples were collected and one
sample had a total coliform positive detect, resulting in 2.33 percent of samples positive. In October
2024, 53 samples were collected and one sample had a total coliform positive detect, resulting in 1.88
percent of samples positive.

The MCL for total coliform is five percent positive samples of all samples per month and the MCLG is
zero. The reason for the total coliform drinking water standard is to minimize the possibility of the water
containing pathogens, which are organisms that cause waterborne disease. Because total coliform is only
a surrogate indicator of the potential presence of pathogens, it is not possible to state a specific numerical
health risk. While USEPA normally sets MCLGs “at a level where no known or anticipated adverse
effects on persons would occur”, they indicate that they cannot do so with total coliforms.

Total coliform bacteria are an indicator organism that are ubiquitous in nature and are not generally
considered harmful. They are used because of the ease in monitoring and analysis. If a positive sample
is found, it indicates a potential problem that needs to be investigated and follow up sampling done. It is
not at all unusual for a system to have an occasional positive sample. It is difficult, if not impossible, to
assure that a system will never get a positive total coliform sample.

The District already implements conventional filtration and adds chlorine to our treated surface water to
assure that the water served to consumers has the least microbiological risk. The chlorine residual levels
are carefully controlled to provide the best health protection without causing the water to have
undesirable taste and odor or increasing the risk caused by higher disinfection byproduct levels. This
careful balance of treatment processes is essential to continue supplying our customers with potable
drinking water. Other equally important measures that we have implemented include: an effective cross-
connection control program, maintenance of a disinfectant residual throughout our system, an effective
monitoring and surveillance program, and maintaining positive pressures in our distribution system. Our
system has already taken all of the steps described by DDW as “best available technology” for coliform
bacteria in Section 64447, Title 22, CCR.

Copper:

There is no MCL for copper. Instead, the 90th percentile value of all samples from house hold taps in the
distribution system cannot exceed an Action Level of 1.3 mg/I for copper. The PHG for copper is 0.3 mg/I.
The category of health risk for copper is gastrointestinal irritation. Numerical health risk data copper has
not yet been provided by OEHHA, the State agency responsible for providing that information.

The source water samples for copper in 2022 and 2025 were non-detectable, therefore less than the
PHG. Based on extensive sampling of our distribution system in 2024, our 90th percentile value for
copper was 0.35 mg/I. This is well below the Action Level of 1.3 mg/L, therefore our water system is in
full compliance with the Federal and State Lead and Copper Rule. Based on our extensive sampling, it
was determined according to State regulatory requirements that we meet the Action Levels for copper.
Therefore, we are deemed by DDW to have “optimized corrosion control” for our system.

In general, optimizing corrosion control is considered to be the “best available technology” to deal with
corrosion issues and with any lead or copper findings. We continue to monitor our water quality

   Subject:        Kokila Reservoir Replacement Project – Construction Contract

   RECOMMENDED ACTION
   Staff requests a Board motion to authorize the Director of Engineering Services to sign a
   construction contract with Gateway Pacific Contractors, Inc. (Gateway) in the amount of
   $10,222,888 with a construction contingency of $1,022,289 (10%) for a total authorized budget
   of $11,245,177. The staff recommendation was reviewed by the Engineering Committee, which
   recommends approval by the Board of Directors.

   BACKGROUND
   The existing Kokila Reservoir is a 4.56 million gallon earthen embankment storage reservoir
   located in the northern portion of the Bacon Pressure Zone and has a 45-mil thickness
   Hypalon membrane liner and cover that protects the stored potable water supply. Originally
   installed in 1984, the liner and cover system has reached the end of its useful life and is in
   need of replacement. This project includes replacing the existing Hypalon lined and covered
   reservoir with a partially buried 4.0 million gallon pre-stressed concrete tank.

   CURRENT STATUS
   The Project was advertised for bidding on May 14, 2025. Thirteen (13) prime contractors, five
   (5) subcontractors, and (1) consultant attended the mandatory pre-bid conference on May 22,
   2025. Bids for the project were received on July 1, 2025, and are summarized as follows:

           Bidder                                                     Bid Amount
           Gateway Pacific Contractors, Inc.                            $10,222,888
           Myers & Sons Construction LLC                                $13,925,740

   Staff reviewed Gateway’s bid and determined their bid was complete and in order, including
   license, insurance, and bonds. Gateway also submitted documentation (i.e. outreach) and
   completed forms with their bid to meet the Drinking Water State Revolving Fund’s
   Disadvantage Business Enterprise (DBE) requirements.


   FINANCIAL CONSIDERATIONS
   The Project is included in the District’s FY 2025-26 Retail budget. Construction costs for the
   Project are expected to be financed through the Drinking Water State Revolving Fund.
   Additionally, the District has received a $1,250,000 grant through the Environmental Protection
   Agency.

     Date:         July 16, 2025
     Subject:      Retail Financial Plan and Rate Proposal


     RECOMMENDED ACTION
     Consider adoption of Resolution No. 25-10 adopting a four-year schedule of retail water
     rates.

     BACKGROUND
     At the June 12, 2025, Board meeting, the Board decided to eliminate from the FY 2025-
     26 District budget the $5.2 million in potential debt issuance for the groundwater program.
     Also at the June 12 meeting, the Board chose not to approve the proposed rate plan,
     which had been noticed pursuant to the requirements of Article XIII(D) of the California
     Constitution (Proposition 218) in late April, and for which the required hearing was held
     on June 11. The motion approved by the Board at the June 12 meeting was to postpone
     further deliberations on retail rates until the further analysis requested by the Board
     occurred.

     Subsequent to the June 12 Board meeting, staff worked with the rate consultant to update
     the rate structure, to eliminate the $5.2 million in potential debt issuance and related
     capital expense. The resulting financial forecast is shown on slide 5 of the attached
     PowerPoint presentation, which can be compared to the original financial forecast of the
     rate schedule that was included in the Proposition 218 notice, which is slide 4. This
     forecast shows that the elimination of the $5.2 million project and its related debt from the
     financial plan results in a 1% reduction in the proposed rate increase for July 2025, with
     the rest of the rate increases remaining the same as noticed. Projected rate increases in
     FY 2030-31 through FY 2033-34 would be reduced from 3% to 2%. The proposed 4-year
     rate schedule is shown on slide 8, and the resulting bimonthly bill analysis is shown on
     slide 9. This rate schedule is less than the original schedule that was in the Proposition
     218 notice, and could thus be approved by the Board, and become effective immediately.

     The additional further analysis requested by the Board included an update of the inventory
     of ¾” and 1” meters, all of which serve single family residential customers. Based on this
     updated inventory, a different weighted average could be calculated, which would be an
     equivalent meter capacity of 46 gallons per minute (compared to the weighted average
     capacity of 38 gallons per minute used for the 2017, 2021 and original 2025 rate
     schedules). Either weighted capacity could be used and still be fully compliant with statute
     and case law. This information is on slide 12 of the PowerPoint, with the associated
     changes to the daily base charge shown on slide 13.

The Board also requested an analysis of a potential usage charge differential for the
gravity zone and the pumped zone, which staff conducted. The electricity and other
operating and maintenance costs for the pump stations were tallied and removed from
the costs that are distributed over the 15% of retail customers in the gravity zone and
shifted instead to the 85% of customers who receive water from the pumping stations.
This rate differential is $0.15/ccf, which is shown in the rate schedule on slide 15 (which
also includes the revised base charge using the revised meter capacity for 1” and below
meters), and the impacts of both of these changes, plus the subtraction of the $5.2 million
from the District’s budget are all shown in the analysis of impacts on the bimonthly bills
on slide 16. The District is not required by statute or case law to create such a rate
differential, but the Board could choose to do so.

The financial forecast associated with these changes in the financial plan is shown on
slide 11. Because the rates in this financial forecast and rate schedule are higher than
those addressed in the current Proposition 218 notice, a new notice would need to be
prepared and circulated to retail customers, a 45-day protest period would be required,
and a new rate hearing would have to be conducted after the end of that 45-day period.

Both rate structures would meet statutory and regulatory requirements. As noted, the
alternative structure would need to go through the new Proposition 218 process. Because
of the confusion and potential controversy associated with a new, higher rate schedule
that shifts more costs to customers in the pumped zone, and which also shifts more costs
to the 95% of San Juan’s retail customers who are single family residential accounts,
staff recommends that the Board approve Resolution 25-10, which adopts the original
four-year rate schedule, as revised to remove the $5.2 million in debt costs.

      WHEREAS, the Board has received and considered the 2024 Retail Water Rate

ensuring that the District’s retail water rates and charges are sufficient to meet revenue
needs for the next four fiscal years, as further discussed in that study;

       WHEREAS, as discussed in the Rate Study, the District’s existing retail water
service rates, consisting of a daily base charge, water usage charges, and, if applicable,
a daily private fire line charge (“Retail Rates”), are insufficient to pay the operating
expenses of the District's retail water utility operations, to provide for repairs and
replacement of water system works, to pay the principal and interest (including meeting
the contractual debt coverage ratio requirements) on water system indebtedness, and to
provide additional revenues for continuing capital improvements to the retail water supply
system at a sustainable pace;

       WHEREAS, the Rate Study identified the need for, and benefits of, implementing
increases to the District’s Retail Rates so that its property owners and water users
(“Customers”) will pay their full proportionate share of all costs necessary to provide water
service to their parcels;

       WHEREAS, the Rate Study demonstrates that the revenues derived from the
proposed increases in the Retail Rates will not exceed the funds required to provide water
service to the affected parcels;

       WHEREAS, the proposed adjustments to the District’s Retail Rates will help
ensure that the District’s financial and water service obligations can be met with reduced
risk and uncertainty, and as a result of the proposed adjustments to the Retail Rate
structure, Customers will benefit from improved District financial stability and certainty,
thereby providing for more reliable water service;

        WHEREAS, the proposed increases in the Retail Rates described in the Rate
Study are subject to Proposition 218’s notice and hearing requirements provided in Article
XIII D, section 6 of the California Constitution;

       WHEREAS, as set forth in the Rate Study, the amount of the proposed Retail
Rates imposed on any class of customers will not exceed the proportional cost of the
water service attributable to that class in compliance with California law, including section
6(b)(3) of Article XIII D of the California Constitution and Griffith v. Pajaro Valley Water
Management Agency ((2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 586, 601) and other relevant law;

WHAT ARE THE PRIMARY REASONS FOR THE NEW WATER RATES?
The proposed retail water rates are expected to generate the necessary revenues to cover projected cost
increases of the District’s retail operations over each of the next four years. The proposed rates have been
calibrated to maintain substantial alignment between fixed/variable costs and fixed/variable revenues. Alignment
between the District’s cost structure and rate structure reduces the need to raise rates during periods of drought,
when the variable cost of producing and delivering water supplies will drop commensurate with the decline in
usage revenues. This approach provides financial stability to the District and rate certainty to our customers. A
corollary benefit of this approach is that it helps maintain the District’s strong credit rating, thereby reducing the
cost of current and future debt obligations.

HOW DO THESE PROPOSED RATES COMPARE TO OTHER WATER AGENCY RATES?
As you can see in the graph below, San Juan Water District’s current and proposed rates remain highly
competitive with those both in the region and across the state. Comparing rates between water providers is
interesting, but not always useful. Each water agency faces unique advantages and challenges, depending on
the topography, elevation, and population of their service area. In addition, each governing board makes different
decisions on how to maintain and improve their systems. Here are some examples of why rates may differ
between water agencies:
• Elevation differences: The San Juan Water District’s retail service area is at a higher elevation than the
  treatment plant that delivers the water. Therefore, water needs to be pumped to reach your tap. Conversely,
  the Fair Oaks Water District and the Citrus Heights Water District are at a lower elevation than the water
  treatment plant. This means that water is delivered via gravity, with no additional pumping costs.
• Density, or lot sizes: The San Juan Water District’s retail service area is comprised of predominately large
  lots, resulting in a greater amount of pipeline per connection than the Citrus Heights Water District, which
  is more densely populated. This means there are fewer customers to share the costs of maintaining more
  pipelines.
• Desired level of service/maintenance: Some agencies choose to keep rates low by deferring maintenance
  and reducing the capital replacement program in the near term, while others, such as the San Juan Water
  District, strive to follow best practices in system maintenance and improvements, believing that proactive
  maintenance and replacement programs are more affordable to the customers in the long run than a reactive
  approach.

PROTESTING THE PROPOSED WATER RATES
Any property owner of record of a parcel that would be subject to the proposed rates, or any tenant directly liable
for payment of water service fees (i.e., a customer of record), may protest the proposed water rate changes. To be
counted, all protests must:

• Be in writing with an original signature from the property owner of record or tenant directly liable for the
  payment of the water bill.
• Identify the parcel for which the protest is filed, by street address or Assessor’s Parcel Number (APN).
• State that the signer opposes the proposed water rates.
• Be received by San Juan Water District at any time prior to the close of the public hearing that will begin at
  6:30 p.m. on Wednesday, June 11, 2025. A protest may be mailed to the San Juan Water District, or deposited
  in our utility bill drop off box located at 9935 Auburn Folsom Road, Granite Bay, CA 95746, or delivered to
  the same address during regular business hours. Please include the following notation on the front of the
  envelope for any written protest: “ATTN: General Manager, Protest of Proposed Water Rates.”
If the District receives written protests in excess of 50 percent of the parcels receiving water service from the
District, the Board will not adopt the proposed rates. Only one protest will be counted for each parcel. By law,
email, fax, telephone, or oral protests of any kind will not be counted. Mailed protests received after the close of
the public hearing will not be counted, even if they were postmarked earlier.

Over the course of the next four years, the Board of Directors may increase retail water rates up to the maximum
amount for each year identified in this notice without providing subsequent hearings or a formal opportunity for
protests. Should the Board of Directors decide to increase rates in any given year in an amount greater than that
described in this notice, a similar Proposition 218 notice will be provided to customers along with an opportunity
for parcel owners and tenants to formally protest such an increase at that time.

Please be advised that pursuant to Government Code Section 53759(d) there is a 120-day statute of limitation for
any challenge to the new, increased or extended fee or charge.

        The District’s Existing and Proposed Rates. The District’s existing and proposed
rates distinguish among single-family residential, commercial (including multi-family
residential uses of more than 4 units), fire lines, temporary, construction, and out-of-
district water sales. Only the proportional cost of service requirement has drawn recent
discussion — the District complies with the total-cost and use-of-proceeds requirements.
Current rates do not recover the cost of service, and the proposed rates fund less than the
capital improvement plan staff initially recommended.


        The District allocates its net revenue requirement (total estimated cost of service
less non-rate revenues such as interest, property taxes, and grants) among customer
classes based on potential demand for service. Related to meter size, this is a measure of
each class’s share of the potential instantaneous demand on the District’s system — a
common means to allocate the cost to build and maintain a water service sufficient to
reliably meet that demand. The allocation to the residential class is based on an estimate
of the mix of meter sizes in use as among 5/8”, ¾” and 1-inch meters. This is permissible
but perhaps more conservative (legally speaking) than is typical. Many agencies simply
treat all single-family uses alike without respect to meter sizes of 1-inch or smaller. This
is because 1-inch meters are typically installed to serve fire sprinklers which, of course,
rarely draw water.


       Within the residential class, the District treats all meters up to 1-inch alike for
purpose of its daily base charge, but imposes a larger charge for larger meters for the
reasons noted above. This is typical. The District charges a uniform volumetric rate for
all water consumed. This is very easily defended and courts which have invalidated
tiered rates as insufficiently justified have assumed uniform rates to calculate refunds.
Distinguishing volumetric rates among customers requires more justification.


      Policy matters. The District could reallocate its discretionary revenues in any way
the Board considers good public policy. It could (but need not) distinguish between
customers in its small gravity zone (15% of customers) from those who rely on pumped
systems (85% of customers). This is because the District is entitled to balance the greater

    II.      Support and Recommendations for New NWP A

ACWA supports USACE’s proposal to issue a new NWP (NWP A) to improve the passage of fish and
other aquatic organisms, as long as it does not adversely impact water quality, supply, reliability, and
other water-related resources. ACWA believes increasing the activities authorized by NWP A would
streamline permitting processes and reduce permitting burdens experienced by some ACWA member
agencies and other public agencies, utilities, tribes, and organizations.

For example, NWP A can expedite water infrastructure activities that many ACWA member agencies
and partners already actively work on. This includes projects across California that pertain to the
passage of fish and other aquatic organisms like , installing fish ladders and fish screens, constructing
bypass channels or nature-like fishways, and modifying infrastructure that restores natural stream
functions. While many of these activities can take a few months to several years to compete (depending
on the project's complexity and scale), NWP A can decrease project timelines.

As NWP A will be a newly established permit, ACWA encourages USACE to properly inform and
equip interested parties with the tools and resources to utilize NWP A once finalized. Considerations

for USACE to consider include direct engagement with public water agencies—such as ACWA
member agencies—and offering training workshops to ensure consistent application in USACE
Divisions and Districts nationwide.

ACWA also notes that NWPs are issued for activities that have minimal adverse effects on the aquatic
environment. However, NWP A involves activities related to fish passage and as such listed species or
critical habitat under the Endangered Species Act (ESA) may be affected. USACE consultation with
the U.S. Fish and Wildfire Service (USFWS) or the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) (also
known as NOAA Fisheries) may therefore be required pursuant to ESA Section 73. While ACWA
supports federal interagency coordination to facilitate project approvals, ESA requirements may
counter USACE intentions to accelerate activities related to NWP A. To avoid potential NWP A project
delays due to ESA requirements, ACWA encourages USACE to specify examples of NWP A activities
that will and may trigger ESA Section 7 requirements in the final rule, as well as issue relevant
guidance. This can help inform NWP A applicants of potential timelines to plan and implement
relevant projects.

    III.      Considerations to Improve USACE’s NWP Program

ACWA reemphasizes that NWPs can benefit water and water-related resources by expediting projects
that may have been delayed due to other permitting process requirements. In implementing NWPs,
ACWA urges USACE to do so in a manner that promotes clear and consistent permitting standards for
ACWA member agencies and other water managers. Certainty throughout the NWP process can
promote efficient water planning to effectively manage critical water infrastructure. This includes
consistent interpretation and application of NWPs across all USACE districts and divisions. As
mentioned above, California has a wide range of water landscapes within the state, but water
landscapes differ nationwide. To avoid varying interpretations between USACE divisions and districts
in NWP processing, USACE should issue guidance and provide training opportunities for agency staff
regardless of geographic location.

When implementing NWPs, ACWA also urges USACE to ensure it does not add unnecessary costs to
water infrastructure projects or unnecessarily delay the permitting process.

Regarding NWP 59 specifically, ACWA urges USACE to remove the ½ acre limitation for this NWP.
ACWA member agencies utilize NWP 59 to improve water quality for the construction, expansion,
and maintenance of water reclamation and reuse facilities and features. NWP 59 accelerates activities
that allow water to be treated and reused for beneficial purposes such as agriculture and irrigation,
potable water supplies, and groundwater replenishment. USACE’s proposed acreage limitation
severely limits the practical use of NWP 59 since most eligible reuse and recharge projects are more
than ½ acre to provide sufficient benefits. These beneficial projects are distinct from other land
development focused NWPs that contain a ½ acre limitation (e.g., NWP 29 Residential Developments,
NWP 39 Commercial and Institutional Developments, and NWP 40 Agricultural Activities). If USACE
maintains an acreage limitation for NWP 59 in the final rule, ACWA requests that the limitation be
expanded to one acre or more.

A strong and capable USACE workforce is the foundation of an effective Nationwide Permit Program.
ACWA recognizes the importance of efficient federal operations. However, we are concerned that the
administration’s reduction of USACE personnel could create challenges for water providers and others

    IV.    Extension Request for Public Comment Period

ACWA appreciates USACE providing an opportunity to comment on its NWPs. However, USACE
provided a remarkably short time for the public to submit written comments. Due to the technical
nature of this proposed rule and its large-scale implications on water resources nationwide, ACWA
encourages USACE to extend this comment period by at least an additional 30 days. Extending the
comment period to a minimum of 60 days will allow USACE to consider adequate responses from
more impacted parties.

The current 30-day timeframe is a challenge for many small water utilities—including some ACWA
member agencies—who have limited capacity and often lack the resources to develop sufficient
comments within the imposed deadline. Nevertheless, those directly affected by activities authorized
by NWPs have important real-world information to share that will likely be hindered by this short
timeframe. Additionally, ACWA has noticed a 30-day comment period for most recent federal
rulemakings, which is significantly shorter compared to standard public comment periods of 60 days.
The flurry of proposed regulations has created numerous overlap between comment deadlines,
oftentimes making it difficult for the regulated community to provide detailed input.
