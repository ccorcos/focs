4. Elimination of January 1, 2026, Retail Water Rate Increase (R)
   Ms. Silva reviewed the written staff report which was included in the Board packet.
   She explained that the rates that were approved at the Board meeting on July 16,
   2025, were based on findings of the 2024 Retail Water Rate Study Report which
   incorporated a long-term financial plan and cost of service analysis generally based
   on the Fiscal Year 2024-25 adopted budget. At the Board’s direction, staff worked
   with the District’s rate consultant to update the financial model using FY 2024-25
   actual results, rather than budgeted amounts. Actual revenues exceeded those
   anticipated in budget and actual expenses were lower than those expected in the
   budget, which reduced the need for rate increases.

   Ms. Silva explained that assuming the financial plan assumptions hold steady into
   the future and that there is no impact to rate revenues from any potential changes to
   the rate structure, the additional $2.1 million net income per year that exceeded the
   assumptions has a substantial positive impact on the District’s reserves over the 10-
   year forecast period. She explained that the District could eliminate the January 2026
                                                              December 17, 2025, Board Minutes
                                                                                       Page 4


   rate increases, build the needed improvements to the Administration Building and
   likely cash fund both the Kokila Reservoir and the Cavitt Stallman Pipeline,
   effectively achieving full “pay-go” status. In addition, she informed the Board that she
   intends to perform this analysis each year in order to ensure approved rate increases
   align with the actual results.

   The Board discussed the elimination of the January 1, 2026, retail water rate
   increase. The Board commended staff for their work on this topic.

   Director Tobin moved to adopt Resolution 25-15 eliminating the January 1,
   2026, retail rate increases. Vice President McRae seconded the motion, and it
   carried unanimously.

5. Retail Rate Structure Analysis (R)
   GM Larsen informed the Board that, in light of Ms. Silva’s most recent report and the
   success of the current rate structure, Director Zamorano requested that the Board
   discuss and consider whether initiating a retail rate structure analysis is necessary.
   He pointed out that, with the positive financial performance and proven effectiveness
   of the current rate structure, the District has achieved pay as you go status.

   In response to Director Tobin’s question, Mr. Zlotnick commented that the District
   just completed a rate study and will be completing another one in 2-3 years, and,
   each year until then, the Board has the ability to lower rate increases if recommended
   by staff and the consultant, therefore, it does not make sense to pursue another
   study at this time.

   Director Zamorano moved to eliminate the retail rate structure analysis project
   and cancel the January 14th workshop. Director Machado seconded the
   motion.

   President Costa commented that when the new idea was presented on the rate
   structure, staff indicated that it would take 6 months or so to research, however, this
   took longer and now it looks like the issue was solved and the workshop isn’t needed.
   In addition, he stated that when the process is started again, then the new idea can
   be considered.

   Vice President McRae reviewed the document that he provided to the Clerk of the
   Board, which was added to the board packet. His document references another
   water agency’s rate study and the California Constitution. He discussed that
   agency’s variable service charge based on meter size, an elevation surcharge, a
   drought surcharge, tiered rates, and the Prop 218 language. He stated that the Board
   should have awareness that there is a difference in costs of delivering water across
   the District. He voiced concern regarding the District’s rates and compliance with
   Prop 218.

   President Costa explained that even after an initiative is approved by voters, it can
   still be repealed or modified by the courts. Court rulings play a critical role in shaping
   the initiative because, unlike laws passed by the legislature, which undergo hearings

and compromises, initiatives bypass that process before adoption. President Costa
stated that instead of charging everyone for various zones and pressures, the Board
decided to use the “postage stamp” method of setting rates for everyone.

minutes of February 19, 2025, which provided the Finance Committee with direction
to create a roadmap guideline to get to a solution regarding the retail water rate

that customers with smaller meters are still paying an unfair higher amount while the
1” meter is getting a discounted rate. She reviewed some court cases regarding

Ms. Deshmane stated that they waited for the Board to discuss the rates and

take this to court and that the Board waited until after that to state that there is no
need for a rate review.

Mr. Kevin Furry addressed the Board and stated that he reviewed a lot of the
challenges of various districts and Prop 218 puts a cap on how much of costs you

unfairly beneficial between him and his neighbor. He explained that he used 20,000

A member of the public addressed the Board and commented that he sees no

Legal Counsel Jones commented that the Board hired two Prop 218 experts who

   STAFF REPORT
   To:           Board of Directors
   From:         Greg Zlotnick, Director of Water Resources & Strategic Affairs

   Date:         January 21, 2026
   Subject:      New Long Term Warren Act Contract


   RECOMMENDED ACTION

   Staff recommends the Board approve Resolution 26-02 and Resolution 26-03 authorizing the
   General Manager to sign both a new Long Term Warren Act Contract (LTWAC) with the
   United States Bureau of Reclamation (Reclamation), so long as it is substantially similar to
   the District’s current Warren Act Contract (WAC) and a 1-year extension of the District’s
   current WAC in case it needs to be in place to allow for continued deliveries of the District’s
   Placer County Water Agency (PCWA) entitlement water until such time that the new LTWAC
   may be executed by the District and Reclamation.

   Further, staff recommends that the Board approve a CEQA Notice of Exemption (NOE) for
   each of the aforementioned resolutions.

   Counsel recommends that each Resolution and NOE be voted on separately in the following
   order:

           [1]   NOE for LTWAC
           [2]   Resolution 26-02 authorizing the General Manager to sign the LTWAC
           [3]   NOE for the 1-year extension of the District’s current WAC
           [4]   Resolution 26-03 authorizing the General Manager to sign a 1-year extension of
                 the District’s current WAC

   BACKGROUND

   A WAC is required for Reclamation to be able to convey non-federal water through federal
   facilities, such as the District’s PCWA entitlement water that is delivered to the District
   through Reclamation’s Central Valley Project facilities at Folsom Dam.

   The District had a 25-year LTWAC that expired February 28, 2021. Under Reclamation’s
   guidance at the time, District staff initiated the process to secure a new 25-year LTWAC in
   2019. However, Reclamation eventually communicated that it would be unable to complete
   the process in a timely manner, which resulted in the District and Reclamation entering into a
   5-year short-term WAC instead. That current 5-year WAC expires February 28, 2026.
Staff Report – New Long Term Warren Act Contract                                 p. 2
January 21, 2026

In 2023, District staff initiated the process with Reclamation to secure a new 25-year LTWAC.
Rather than relying on Reclamation to undertake development of the required environmental
documentation (i.e., an “Environmental Assessment” or “EA”), the District retained and
engaged a consultant in May of that year to develop the EA to comply with National
Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) requirements for Reclamation to enter into a new LTWAC
with the District.

In February of 2024, as a result of some uncertainties related to the timely availability of
pertinent modeling information, District staff requested and received a written timeline
providing assurance that Reclamation and its federal agency partners, in collaboration with
the District’s consultant, would complete the EA by the end of August 2025 and the new
LTWAC would be able to be executed no later than December 31, 2025.

Unfortunately, broad federal agency staffing reductions last year, as well as the federal
government shutdown last fall, prevented Reclamation from meeting that timeline. The NEPA
documentation was only completed about a week ago, and as of the date of the writing of this
staff report, it is being required to be reviewed by the Commissioner of Reclamation in
Washington D.C., and potentially too by the Assistant Secretary of the Interior for Water and
Science, who oversees Reclamation. This level of review has generally not been required in
the past. Consequently, there is a high level of uncertainty that the EA will be finalized in time
for the Board to approve the new LTWAC at its February 18th meeting.

When District staff was informed late last year of the potential for this level of review being
required, Reclamation was requested to prepare for the contingency of not having the new
LTWAC ready for execution by February 28th. Reclamation responded by stating a 1-year
extension of the District’s current WAC would be provided to allow the District to continue
accessing its PCWA entitlement water until the LTWAC could be executed.

To provide the District with the most flexibility in light of the uncertainty of the timing of the
availability of the new LTWAC, and because there can be no assurance that the LTWAC will
be available for Board approval at its February meeting, District staff are requesting the
Board to authorize the General Manager to sign (i) the new LTWAC, so long as it is
determined by the District’s Director of Water Resources and Strategic Affairs, in consultation
with the District’s water counsel, as being substantially similar to the District’s current WAC;
and, (ii) a 1-year extension of the District’s current WAC if it is necessary to maintain interim
delivery capability of the District’s PCWA supplies.

Reclamation has advised District staff that it will accept the General Manager as signatory for
the District for both documents if authorized to do so by Board resolution.

The Board’s consideration and potential approval of Resolutions to authorize the General
Manager to sign both the LTWAC and a 1-year extension of the current WAC are “covered
actions” subject to CEQA. For that reason, the Board also needs to approve CEQA Notices of
Exemption for each Resolution, which will be publicly posted and advertised as legally
required.

to the existing Warren Act contract and to allow for the conveyance of up to 25,000 acre-feet
annually of non-Project water (water not developed as part of the Central Valley Project) from
the Placer County Water Agency’s Middle Fork Project through Folsom Reservoir and the
federal facilities at Folsom Dam in all water years; and

        WHEREAS, despite the District staff’s diligent efforts, the finalization of the new
LTWAC has been significantly delayed, in part due to the shutdown of the federal government in
the Fall of 2025, and the new LTWAC may not be finalized for execution prior to the District
Board’s regular February 2026 Board meeting; and

        WHEREAS, in order to avoid delay in execution of the LTWAC, if the LTWAC is not

District Board desires to authorize the General Manager to execute the LTWAC.

      Affairs and the District’s water counsel shall review the final LTWAC and determine
      whether the LTWAC is substantially similar to the existing Warren Act contract between
      the District and the Bureau.

   3. If the LTWAC is substantially similar to the existing Warren Act contract based on the
      review described in paragraph 2, the General Manager of the San Juan Water District is
      hereby authorized to sign the LTWAC.

   4. If the LTWAC is not substantially similar to the existing Warren Act contract, the
      LTWAC shall be presented to the District Board for review and consideration at a future

     Discussion under this item is intended to be limited to procurement approach and

     BACKGROUND
     At the December 17, 2025, Board meeting, Director Tobin requested that the Board
     consider whether to discuss issuing an RFP for General Counsel services. As with other
     contracted professional services, Boards periodically review procurement approaches to
     ensure they remain consistent with governance objectives, transparency considerations,
     and operational needs.

     decision to issue, or not issue, an RFP is discretionary and may involve consideration of
     multiple factors, including continuity of service, institutional knowledge, responsiveness,
     cost structure, and market conditions.

     procurement options. It does not evaluate, comment on, or imply any concerns
     regarding the performance of current legal counsel. Any discussion of legal counsel
     performance would be inappropriate in public session, but could be done in closed
     session if that is the will of the Board.

     SUMMARY

     to pursue an RFP process for General Counsel services.

RE: SUPPORT – AB 35 (Alvarez): Proposition 4 Administrative Procedure Act (APA)
Exemption
SET FOR JANUARY 22, 2026

Dear Chair Wicks,

On behalf of the undersigned organizations, we write in strong support of AB 35 (Alvarez),
which would exempt Proposition 4 spending from the Administrative Procedure Act (APA). The
signatures below represent a broad community of stakeholders, including many environmentally
focused organizations that played a critical role in advocating for Proposition 4’s passage on the
November 2024 ballot.

Exempting Proposition 4 funding from the APA is consistent with long standing precedent for
natural resource bonds, including Proposition 84 and Proposition 68. A full exemption would
enable funds to be deployed more quickly and efficiently, ensuring that protracted administrative
processes do not hold up vital community projects. Agencies such as the State Water Resources
Control Board (SWRCB), the Department of Water Resources and the Department of Food and
Agriculture have noted that a standard process, complete with review by the Office of
Administrative Law (OAL), could result in delays of 12 to 18 months before communities
receive awards. These delays run counter to overwhelming support of the voters for Proposition
4 and their desire to see needed projects start in a timely manner. In fact, there are pressing
issues such as border rivers, groundwater recharge, water recycling, and wildfire prevention that
require immediate funding. Existing programs were created using the processes dictated by prior
bonds. Hence, the programs most able to spend funds quickly will be forced to needlessly
restructure them to meet the regulatory requirement established by the September Appropriations
bill. For example, the SWRCB’s drinking water funding program will be adversely impacted
since under the current rules, all funding sources within the program are covered under the
SWRCB’s Intended Use Plan (IUP), which allows flexibility in which sources of funding are
used for different projects, and the IUP includes Proposition 4 funds. Without the exemption
from the APA process, the Prop 4 funding would have to be backed out of the IUP and limit the
ability to use a broad mix of funding to achieve the State’s goals of providing a safe and reliable
drinking water supply.

Importantly, an exemption from the APA does not eliminate transparency, consultation, or public
engagement. Agencies will continue to follow robust fiscal, reporting, and oversight
requirements. Many agencies have already gone through extensive public processes for these
existing programs. Instead, the exemption will streamline implementation, maintain consistency
across programs, and avoid creating barriers to delivering critical funding where it is most
needed.
Without an APA exemption, the delays already emerging threaten the success of essential
projects across the state, including many that are shovel ready. Providing this exemption will
accelerate timelines, reduce administrative burdens, and honor the commitment voters made to
California’s communities.

For these reasons, we strongly support a full exemption from the APA for all Proposition 4
funding.
