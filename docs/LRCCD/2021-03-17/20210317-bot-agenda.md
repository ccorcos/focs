Chancellor’s Report:

ARC: ARC welcomes photographer Matika Wilber on Thursday, February 18, at 7:00 pm as she
presents "Project 562: Changing the Way We See Native America" to the Los Rios community via
Zoom. Project 562 is a multi-year national photography project dedicated to photographing over
562 federally recognized tribes in the United States in an effort to create an unprecedented
repository of imagery and oral histories that accurately portray contemporary Native Americans.

This special event is held in conjunction with the ARC Photography Area, ARC Native American
Resource Center, and Viewpoint Photographic Art Center Student Program and is made possible
by an Innovation Grant from the ARC Philanthropy Office.

CRC: 195 student athletes at CRC recorded a 3.109 cumulative grade-point-average while
attempting 13.22 units for the fall 2020 semester.

Eight CRC programs recorded a team GPA of 3.0 or higher, including a 3.453 mark from CRC
Women's Basketball. Also achieving a 3.0 team GPA or higher was Women's Swimming (3.413),
Women's Tennis (3.397), Women's Volleyball (3.337), Baseball (3.286), Softball (3.103), Men's
Tennis (3.027), and Women's Soccer (3.006).

An astounding total of 23 student-athletes recorded a perfect 4.0 GPA for the fall semester while
attempting 12 or more units, with 9 different teams having at least one 4.0 student-athlete with
12 more units on their roster. Baseball had 7 team members with a 4.0, followed by Women's
Soccer with 4, and Women's Volleyball, Men's Soccer, and Women's Swimming who each had
three.

A program record 95 student-athletes were recognized as scholar-athletes for recording a 3.00
or higher GPA while attempting 12 or more units.

FLC: Last fall, as part of Folsom Lake College’s expansive “equity in action” efforts, the college
launched a campus-wide book read of Ibram X. Kendi’s How to Be an Antiracist, which studied
systemic racism in America and proposed how we can individually and collectively work towards
an antiracist society. This spring, the self-reflection work continues using the text Me and White
Supremacy: Combat Racism, Change the World, and Become a Good Ancestor by Layla F. Saad.
Starting this week, Saad's book will guide FLC Book Circle participants through a step-by-step
process of journaling and small group discussion, encouraging those with white privilege to
examine their conscious and unconscious biases, and then do the necessary and vital work that
can ultimately lead to improving race relations benefiting our greater college community. For
more information on FLC’s equity efforts across the institution, go to:
inside.flc.losrios.edu/equityinaction.

SCC: Across the district, and here at SCC we are celebrating Black History Month with events all
month long, featuring spotlights on Black art, literature, music, wellness, finances and
community. The districtwide Welcome Black Event on Zoom was held last Friday, Feb. 5, allowing
students to mix and mingle with each other and hear from Black college presidents, vice
president, administration and staff. SCC also has a series of events planned, including “Melanated
Music Monday,” “Creative Writing Circle,” “Wisdom, Nutrition and Wellness,” and “Black
Jeopardy.”

BACKGROUND:
Education Code Section 72023.5(a) requires the board of trustees of each community college
district to have at least one non-voting student member to be chosen by the students in a
manner determined by the board. Education Code Section 72023.5(b)(3) provides that the
Student Trustee may receive compensation for board service, at the discretion of the governing
board, up to the amount allowed for elected board members.

Los Rios Board Policies provide for payment to the Student Trustee in two areas: (1) travel
reimbursement/mileage while performing services on behalf of the District; and (2) a monthly
stipend. The Student Trustee is also eligible for District health, medical, and dental insurance
policies through plans adopted by the District.

Los Rios Board Policies provide for the Student Trustee to receive reimbursement for mileage in
the same manner and to the same extent as other Board of Trustee members. Los Rios Board
Policies set the Student Trustee stipend at seventy-five percent (75%) of the stipend received
by elected members of the Board, and specifically except the Student Trustee from receiving
the full amount allowed under the Education Code.

STATUS:
The proposed Policy amendments, if adopted, would provide the Student Trustee with a monthly
stipend equal to other members of the Los Rios Board of Trustees. The Student Trustee would
continue to be eligible for District health, medical and dental insurance and travel
reimbursement/mileage in the same manner and to the same extent as other Board of Trustee
members.

RECOMMENDATION:
It is recommended that the Board of Trustees approve the proposed revisions to the attached
policies.

BACKGROUND:
In August of 2019, the Natomas Unified School District (NUSD) acquired a 5.788-acre parcel
adjacent to Inderkum High School, the North Natomas Public Library, and the American River
College Natomas Center (Natomas Center). The Los Rios Community College District needs
additional parking for students and employees at the Natomas Center, and the NUSD is
supportive of continued and expanded Los Rios programming in the Natomas community,
including college and career ready programs for high school students. In the spirit of continued
collaboration and in support of educational opportunities for the Natomas community, NUSD has
agreed to enter into a fifty-five (55) year ground lease with the Los Rios Community College
District which would allow the District to construct and operate a parking lot on a portion of the
identified parcel consisting of approximately 285 new parking spaces. In exchange, the District
will continue collaboration and expand services offered to high school students in the Natomas
community.

Representatives of the District and NUSD have worked over the last several months to negotiate
the terms of the proposed ground lease, which includes the following major terms:
    • 55 year ground lease, with option to extend or renew with mutual agreement
    • The District will fund, construct, and operate the parking lot
    • The District is responsible for required plans, approvals, and tie-ins
    • The District will set parking fees and fines consistent with District policies
    • The District will have control of approximately 285 parking spaces, with the exception of
       6 spaces that will be reserved for NUSD maintenance crews and personnel
    • NUSD and the District will enter into a separate partnership agreement that will be
       beneficial to the goals of the District and NUSD to support students obtaining higher
       education

BACKGROUND:
Each year at their May meeting, the Board of Governors of California Community Colleges honor
classified employees who demonstrate the highest level of commitment to professionalism and
community colleges. Recipients are nominated by their colleagues and endorsed by the local
Board of Trustees. Award winners are selected by representatives of the Community College
Board of Governors, Chancellor’s Office, and the Foundation for California Community Colleges.

STATUS:
Each local Board of Trustees may forward to the California Community Colleges Chancellor’s
Office the name of one nominee for the Community College Classified Employee of the Year
Award. This year the District is recommending the nomination of Clarissa Correa, Student
Personnel Assistant (CRC).

Clarissa has worked as a full-time employee within the Los Rios Community College District since
2006 where she began her time with Los Rios in the Small Business Development Center (SBDC).
While with SBDC, she assisted clients through various tasks related to preparing and completing
business plans, marketing plans, financial statements and grant applications. Clarissa assisted
with identifying over 15 funding opportunities for the Center and the Region, which allows the
Center to apply for over $600,000 in grants and contracts in order to meet the Small Business
Administration Federal grant requirements. In 2015, Clarissa transferred to Cosumnes River
College as a result of the Small Business Development Center contract not being renewed.

The Assessment Center welcomed Clarissa to the team and Clarissa quickly became part of the
Student Support Center family which included EOPS/CARE, CalWORKs and Assessment. As the
Student Personnel Assistant for Assessment, Clarissa has provided support through the many
changes and areas of ambiguity as CRC implemented AB705. System-wide,
Assessment/Placement has undergone a thorough interrogation of the validity of using a test to
place students, and the unintended consequences of using a test. Following this, there were
changes mandated to State law.

Upon full District implementation of AB705, course placements were made available in eServices
for students. The team had very little training on how information would be displayed to
                                                                                                 76
                                                                                                       HOME
students. Clarissa took the lead with investigating and disseminating information, working closely
with instructional faculty; namely, the Math and English department chairs. She served as the
voice of our students, by communicating areas of confusion and frustration that students were
experiencing. She focused on solutions, and transferred complex information into easy-to-read
charts that students, faculty and staff could understand. At the many complex junctures, she took
initiative to find the answers and anticipate technological barriers that students would
encounter.

To respond effectively to this dynamic change required flexibility, resilience, attention to detail,
awareness of global impact, and an unwavering focus on the reason for the change: to support
student success. Clarissa embodies and practices all of these qualities, with equity at the center
of it all. She understands that creating an inclusive and equitable learning and work environment
is important for many reasons. She is grateful for the opportunity to work closely with students
from various backgrounds; and takes extra time to provide assistance to students as they
navigate the college process so that they can be successful here at CRC and reach their academic
goals. CRC is lucky to have Clarissa Correa as part of our team and it is our honor to nominate her
for the Community College Employee of the Year Award.

RECOMMENDATION:
It is recommended that the Board of Trustees endorse the nomination of Clarissa Correa, of
Cosumnes River College, for the 2021 California Community Colleges Classified Employee of the
Year Award.

BACKGROUND:
With the passage of Assembly Bill 288, California community college districts are authorized to
enter into College and Career Access Pathways Partnerships with high school districts to expand
opportunities for high school students to take college courses during the regular school day and
for community colleges to claim apportionment. To go forward with this program, the law
requires community college districts and high school districts to enter into a Memorandum of
Understanding approved by the governing boards of both the community college district and
the high school district.

STATUS:
The proposed College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) Memorandum of Understanding
(MOU) is between the Los Rios Community College District and Westlake Charter School. The
purpose of the MOU is to offer and expand dual enrollment opportunities for students who
may not already be college bound or who are underrepresented in higher education, with the
goal of developing seamless pathways from high school to community college. Beginning Fall
2022, classes will be offered to High School students Westlake Charter School. At the
completion of these classes, students will earn credit toward multiple degree or certificate
programs within the ARC catalog.

CCAP MOU between the Los Rios Community College District and Westlake Charter School and

         Upon completion of this course, the student will be able to:

               assess student success strategies and identify techniques and campus resources to build effective learning skills in areas such as: textbook reading, note
               taking, memory skills, exam preparation, and exam taking.
               distinguish among different learning styles (i.e. auditory, visual, kinesthetic, and/or read/write) in identifying personal learning preference(s).
               locate and identify campus programs and support services.
               incorporate campus rules and requirements for degree and transfer planning.
               analyze and choose effective communication skills that apply to the academic, career, and/or personal life issues.
               demonstrate measurable goal-setting and decision-making skills.
               recognize the various elements of diversity on our campus.
               demonstrate problem-solving and critical-thinking skills.
               demonstrate personal wellness skills such as stress reduction and health maintenance.
               discriminate between effective and non-effective goal and life planning skills.
               formulate and implement appropriate interpersonal skills and conflict resolution strategies.
               appraise and select the most appropriate modes of instruction for personal learning success.

          Hours:          54 hours lecture, 108 hours out-of-class work, for a total of 162 student learning hours.
          Description: This interdisciplinary course introduces students to the theoretical and practical foundations of social justice and the social processes that
                       create and resist oppression. It covers the sociology, history, and psychology of oppressions based upon race, ethnicity, class, gender,
                       sexuality, and other group identities in the United States and the corresponding social justice movements for liberation. It investigates how
                       creating and undoing asymmetrical power relations are linked to social structures, institutional processes, and culture. Additionally, it
                       provides a basis for a better understanding of socioeconomic, political, and cultural conditions of key social groups in the United States.
                       Topics include theoretical foundations of social justice and oppression, history and politics of group identity, culture and ideologies, forms
                       of oppression, privilege, and forms of resistance. Field trips may be required.


         Section 3: Learning Outcomes and Objectives

         Upon completion of this course, the student will be able to:

               define the foundational theories within social justice studies.
               differentiate between oppression and other forms of mistreatment.
               explain power asymmetry as it is linked to social structure and institutional processes.
               analyze theories on how identity is created for groups in a society via power, economic, political, and cultural structures.
               summarize the modern histories and experiences of groups oppressed because of race, ethnicity, class, gender, and sexuality in the United States.
               compile contributions of oppressed groups to the development of American society.
               assess the complex intersections and relationships within and across race, ethnicity, socioeconomic class, gender, sexual orientation, and other
               identities.
               identify and analyze the role of culture in oppression and in empowerment, including art, film, literature, or music reflecting different groups.
               understand theories on how and why groups resist.
               identify struggles for social justice, liberation, and decolonization.
               apply how privilege and oppression operate in the major institutions of the United States, including education, health care, the economy, and the
               criminal justice system.

         Section 5: Methods of Instruction

         Lectures, discussions, interactive learning activities, student reports, guest speakers, media presentations, and field trips.


         Section 6: Typical Student Assignments

         Sample assignment #1:
         In a 3-page essay, analyze the cultural construction of race in a contemporary popular film. How does this film's construction of race either support or
         deconstruct the dominant narrative of race for dominant and oppressed group identity and power? Use academic terms and definitions to set up your analysis.
         (Addresses SLO: identify and analyze the role of culture in oppression and in empowerment, including art, film, literature, or music reflecting different
         groups.)

         Sample assignment #2:
         Working in your assigned teams, select a social group who experiences a marginalized identity in the United States. Construct a timeline for that group that
         includes its initial contact into the United States, important events that create identity for the group (legal, political, economic, cultural), leaders and notable
         lives, and struggles for social justice, liberation, and decolonization. Each person in your team will turn in a 5-page paper on one aspect of their timeline and,
         together, the team will present a synthesized summary of their timeline.
         (Addresses SLO: summarize the modern histories and experiences of groups oppressed because of race, ethnicity, class, gender, and sexuality in the United
         States.)

state governments provided a series of financial aid opportunities for our students.

       Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund (HEERF I).

       was signed into law in December 2020 and provided approximately $24 billion was given
       to high education through the Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund II (HEERF II).

   •   Currently, Congress is attempting to pass another stimulus that may include
       approximately $40 billion to high education through the Higher Education Emergency
       Relief Fund III (HEERF III).

       available for the California Dreamer Service Incentive Grant program, to be used to
       provide disaster relief emergency student financial aid to California community college
       students.

       provide emergency financial assistance to low-income students.
