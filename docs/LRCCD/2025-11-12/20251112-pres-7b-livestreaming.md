     Live-Streaming
Los Rios Board Meetings
     Information item
     November 12, 2025
               Background and Context
●   Neither the Brown Act, nor any other state law requires live streaming of meetings.
●   Districts that stream meetings are not required to allow public comments remotely (few
    districts allow for online comments, as it adds logistical and technological complexity and can
    disproportionately incentivize non-local participants).
●   Los Rios currently records the audio of each meeting, which is available to members of public
    upon request.
●   While the Board’s current practice is transparent and legally compliant, some argue that
    streaming meetings would increase public transparency.
              Background and Context
●   Los Rios has only ever live streamed meetings during the COVID-19 Pandemic, when
    in-person meetings were not possible.
●   Many Community College and K-12 Districts livestream their meetings, and some do not.
     ○   Sierra College, Los Angeles CCD, San Diego CCD, City College of San Francisco,
         Sacramento City Uniﬁed, Elk Grove Uniﬁed, Davis Joint Uniﬁed among those who live
         stream meetings.
     ○   Chabot La Positas CCD, Lake Tahoe Community College, College of Marin, Santa
         Barbara City College, Galt Joint Union High school District among those who do not live
         stream meetings.
           Operational Considerations
●   Los Rios District Ofﬁce Board Room not currently equipped for live streaming.
●   IT estimates a cost of between $160,000-$185,000 to equip room with appropriate video and
    audio equipment for District Ofﬁce, as well as approximately $16,000 in additional annual
    ongoing costs for licences and stafﬁng.
●   College-hosted meetings would not have the same infrastructure for streaming (the cost of
    equipping those sites is not included in the above estimate).
●   Other considerations, while not insurmountable, include the potential for technical difﬁculties
    or delays, ADA compliance (closed captioning), and the risk of inappropriate use of video or
    AI “deep fakes.”
