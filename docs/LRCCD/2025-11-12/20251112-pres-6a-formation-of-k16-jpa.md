              Collaborative Background
●   The Sacramento K-16 Collaborative launched in 2022 with an $18.1 million grant from the
    State of California.
●   It is a multi-county partnership across eight counties (Colusa, El Dorado, Nevada, Placer,
    Sacramento, Sutter, Yolo, Yuba).
●   Partners include: Los Rios, K-12 districts and County Ofﬁces of Education, UC Davis, CSU
    Sacramento, Sierra College and other regional community colleges, and key employers like
    SMUD, Kaiser Permanente, Accenture, and Bosch.
           Building on the Collaborative
As the state grant nears its end, there is a shared regional interest in maintaining the momentum of
the Collaborative’s seminal work by creating a lasting structure to continue:

 ●   Sharing data across educational institutions.
 ●   Supporting students of color, low-income, ﬁrst-generation, and adult learners.
 ●   Building targeted career pathways in health care, engineering, computing, biotech, and other
     growth industries.
 ●   Expanding seamless transfer pathways from high school to community college to university.
 ●   Expanding dual enrollment opportunities.

    What is a Joint Powers Authority
●   The Joint Exercise of Powers Act (California Government Code § 6500 et seq.) authorizes
    public agencies to agree to jointly exercise their common powers and to create a separate
    entity to execute such agreement.
●   The proposed JPA Agreement creates a new public agency called the PK-16 Collaborative
    Joint Powers Authority or “Collaborative JPA” by January 1, 2026.
●   The JPA would be governed by a Board of Directors made up of executive representatives
    from member organizations, including Los Rios.
Why Create a Joint Powers Authority?
A Joint Powers Authority (JPA) creates a dedicated, stand-alone entity to:

 ●   Maintain sustainable data security infrastructure,
 ●   Strengthen regional collaboration in service of shared goals, and
 ●   Pursue new streams of public and private funding.

As written, creation of the JPA presents little-to-no risk to Los Rios:

 ●   NO commitment of ﬁnancial investment today (members could decide to share minimal costs
     if fundraising/grant efforts do not generate enough to cover operating expenses).
 ●   If JPA does not reach critical mass of membership (estimated minimum of 5 members), it can
     be dissolved without penalty.
      Why a JPA, and not a Nonproﬁt?
Unlike a nonproﬁt, a JPA:

 ●   Is a public entity created by and for its member agencies.
 ●   Provides greater accountability, transparency, and legal authority for data sharing.
 ●   Ensures that control of the work remains with education and public partners, rather than an
     external organization, while still enabling pursuit of private and philanthropic funding.
 ●   Allows for a low cost to member agencies (lower overhead than a typical 501(c)3).
                                  Why Now?
●   With the State K-16 Grant sunsetting, failing to build a strong new structure now risks seeing
    the progress we’ve made over the past three years grind to a halt.

●   Los Rios continues to lead this work, and our action tonight would be the ﬁrst to create the
    legal entity (Sierra College is agendizing for their December meeting, Elk Grove Uniﬁed
    considering upcoming opportunities as well).

●   Positions Los Rios for signiﬁcant future investments via regranting opportunities (the
    Collaborative aims to raise $1.5 million dollars by June of 2026, with 11 grant opportunities
    currently being pursued).
