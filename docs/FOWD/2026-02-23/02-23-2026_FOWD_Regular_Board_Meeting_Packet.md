The Board of Directors of the Fair Oaks Water District holds its Regular Board Meetings on the third Monday of
each month at 6:30 p.m. The meetings are held at the District Offices located at 10326 Fair Oaks Blvd., Fair Oaks,
CA 95628. The Board may discuss any item on the agenda and may act on any of those items.

The Board of Directors welcomes public participation in its meetings. Public comments relating to matters within
jurisdiction of the District, and not included on the posted agenda, may be addressed under “public comment,” both
at the beginning and at the end of the meeting, subject to reasonable time limitations for each speaker. Please note
that State law prevents the Board from discussing or acting on items not listed on the agenda. Public comments
relating to matters listed on the agenda may be provided at the time when that agenda item is heard.

Agenda items are numbered for identification purposes only and will not necessarily be considered in the indicated
order. Items appearing on the Consent Calendar are considered routine and may be acted upon by the Board by one
motion, without discussion; however, any item may be considered separately at the request of any Board member or
any member of the public.

In compliance with the American with Disabilities Act, if you have a disability and need a disability-related
modification or accommodation to participate in this meeting, please contact the Human Resource Administrator at
(916) 967-5723. Requests must be made as early as possible, and at least one full business day before the start of the
meeting.

3. Discussion and possible action on FOWD Resolution No. 26-01: “Water Forum
   2050 Agreement”
   • General Manager Gray provided a staff recommendation to approve the FOWD
      Resolution No. 26-01 and read the resolution for the Board.
   • Director Babcock expressed appreciation for the work done on the agreement. He
      stated that he is concerned that the agreement states it is co-equal with water
      districts and other entities because not all are equal. He stated that water districts
      should be more protected. If there is a drought period and to ensure water for our
      customers, there are no protections for that because one of the entities in the
      agreement could sue FOWD. The agreement is not legally binding, and it concerns
      him that it does not mention that people come first.
   • General Manager Gray responded that water districts being sued by the
      environmental caucus and businesses in 2002 is what brought the Water Forum
      Agreement forward. He stated that it brought entities together, while not legally
      binding, to prevent litigation.
   • General Manager Gray stated that the agreement is implemented through mutual
      commitments and if there are multiple years of drought, FOWD will provide water
      to customers first.
   • Director Petersen provided comments on the history of the Water Forum
      Agreement.
   • Director Babcock stated that he is nervous about the agreement not stating that other
      groups will not necessarily acquiesce to the others.
   • General Manager Gray responded that it is an agreement with a level of trust
      between the caucuses. He stated that if others do not follow the intention of the
      agreement, the water caucus will push back.
   • Visitor Joseph stated that he absolutely agrees with General Manager Gray. It is a
      matter of trust built up over decades that goes a long way with state and federal
      funding. They are pleased to hear that there are no disputes or litigation/verge of
      litigation. He stated that RWA continually points to the Water Forum Agreement
      that has enabled them to be successful and advance projects.

   Director Sarkovich moved to approve the FOWD Resolution No. 26-01 as presented.
   President Marx seconded the motion.

   Motion carried with the following votes: Babcock – aye, Dolby – aye, Marx – aye,
   Petersen – aye, and Sarkovich – aye.

equipping of the replacement New York Well. Later in 2019, the contract was amended to
include environmental permitting and design of an ASR system to be incorporated into the
project.
Design and construction for Phase I (well drilling) were completed in 2020. Design for
Phase II (well equipping) began in 2021 but was not finalized until 2025 due to multiple
plan revisions and design resubmittals by KASL. Phase II was advertised for bid in 2025,
and FOWD awarded the construction contract to T&S Construction at the end of 2025.
Construction is scheduled to begin this month, with project completion anticipated in 2027.
Contract and Scope Changes:
When the professional services contract was executed in 2019, it was assumed that Phases
I and II would proceed sequentially, allowing for efficiencies and cost savings. That
assumption did not materialize. Over time, the project scope expanded to include additional
improvements such as modifications to perimeter walls and access gates, site grading and
drainage improvements, and enhanced water quality features.
In addition, the original scope did not include applications programming for the well
control system. This work, which is now required, represents $61,930 of the proposed
contract amendment.
Cost Drivers and Regulatory Changes:
Regulatory standards for permitting and approval of ASR systems have become more
stringent since 2019. Current requirements place greater emphasis on groundwater
modeling, testing, and analysis to prevent adverse water quality impacts to the groundwater



                                                                                    Page 47 of 96
basin. These changes have resulted in additional professional services that were not
contemplated in the original contract.
During the extended design period, KASL utilized portions of the original contract that had
been reserved for construction management, permitting, and ASR testing to complete
revised design efforts. As a result, the original contract was fully expended by the time
Phase II was bid, leaving several required services outstanding.
Consultant Accountability and Cost Offsets:
The revised proposal reflects updated charge-out rates for KASL and its subconsultants,
which have increased approximately 29 percent since 2019. This increase is consistent
with broader market increases in construction and professional services during the same
period.
To acknowledge responsibility for delays in producing bid-ready plans, KASL reduced its
proposed amendment by $53,000, effectively absorbing a portion of these costs. KASL has
also eliminated subconsultant markups for all ASR-related services, further mitigating the
overall cost increase.
Request for Additional Funding:
The original 2019 professional services contract for design, construction management, and
ASR improvements totaled $382,068. The proposed amendment of $367,000 reflects the
significant expansion of scope, regulatory complexity, and project duration since contract
execution.

(C26WTNYWDE). Approval of the contract amendment will therefore require an
additional appropriation of $367,000 to support construction and avoid project delays.

Urban Water Management Plans (UWMPs) are prepared by all California urban water
suppliers to support long-term water resource planning and to ensure that adequate water
supplies are available to meet both current and future demands. UWMPs are updated
every five years, adopted by the District Board, and submitted to the California
Department of Water Resources (DWR).
Urban water suppliers that provide more than 3,000 acre-feet of water annually, or that
serve more than 3,000 urban service connections, are required to evaluate the reliability
of their water supplies over a 20-year planning horizon. UWMPs also describe
demand-management measures and water shortage contingency strategies, report
progress toward the State’s targeted reduction in per-capita urban water use and outline
existing and planned uses of recycled water.
To meet these statutory requirements, FOWD staff initiated a process for preparation of
the 2025 Urban Water Management Plan. A Request for Proposals (RFP) to hire a
consultant to complete the work was posted on the FOWD website and distributed to
multiple local consulting firms on January 6, 2025.
Proposals submitted by the January 30, 2026 deadline were reviewed and evaluated based
on the following criteria:
      •   Responsiveness to the RFP
      •   Firm experience and qualifications
      •   Proposed schedule for completion
      •   Demonstrated project understanding
Based on this evaluation, the consulting firm Verdantas received the highest overall
score.
